\section{Setting and reforms}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Question and headline}
\footnotesize
\textbf{Setting.} Sequential electricity wholesale markets. Spain moved the trading time resolution from 60 minutes to 15 minutes in two staggered reforms in 2025: spot markets in March, forward market in October.

\medskip
\textbf{Question.} Does finer time resolution reshape strategic bidding? Does market power go up or down? What is the effect on prices, market differentials, and imbalance penalties?

\medskip
\fcolorbox{green!45!black}{green!7}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.96\linewidth}\vspace{2pt}
\textbf{\color{green!40!black}Answer.} Granularity made the market \emph{more competitive without changing aggregate quantities}.
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
  \item \textbf{Prices.} March reform reduced prices substantially, October one no.
  \item \textbf{Market power.} Residual demand becomes more elastic for dominant firms at every reform.
  \item \textbf{Bid-curves.} Important changes in shape but mixed evidence, renewables compress their ladders and conventional grade them more finely in periods of the day with electricity ramps.
  \item \textbf{Imbalance.} Penalty per deviating for schedule drops by a third in march --- same volume of deviation, resolved cheapely.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{2pt}\end{minipage}}

\medskip
\textbf{Caveat.} An April 2025 blackout triggered a separate regulatory regime ("operaci\'on reforzada") that inflated the ancillary-services cost line. Granularity and that regime are calendar-overlapping but mechanistically distinct.
\end{frame}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{frame}{The institutional setting --- sequential electricity wholesale markets}
\bigskip
\textbf{The product.} Delivery of MW (rate) over a fixed short time slot, by different technologies.

\bigskip
\fcolorbox{blue!50!black}{blue!7}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.96\linewidth}\vspace{2pt}
\textbf{\color{blue!50!black}Sequence of markets.} Electricity cannot be stored cheaply, supply must equal demand in real time. For this reason, we have sequential \textbf{wholesale} markets before delivery:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
  \item \textbf{Forward auction (``day-ahead'', DA)} --- clears the day before. Biggest market, with the most liquidity.
  \item \textbf{Spot auctions (``intraday auctions'', IDA)} --- clears closer to delivery. Corrections.
  \item \textbf{Balancing (real time)} --- corrections by the system operator (REE). Highly complex, with separate pricing mechanisms.
\end{itemize}
Both DA and IDA are \textbf{uniform-price multi-unit auctions}; bidders submit step-function supply and demand curves and price is set at the intersection of the aggregate supply and demand curves, using a complex algorithm that maximizes social welfare.
\vspace{2pt}\end{minipage}}

\bigskip
\textbf{Huge complexity:} ancillary markets, bilateral contracts, cross-border trading, continuous intraday markets, etc. We focus on DA and IDA, and present basic descriptive statistics on some of the rest.

\end{frame}

\section{Mechanisms and identification strategy}

\begin{frame}{A priori expected mechanisms from the reform sequence}
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
  \item This is not the multi-stage market setup of \citet{allaz_vila_1993}. The closest paper is \citet{ito_reguant_2016}, which studies arbitrage between forward and spot markets.
  \item Our setup is \citet{ito_reguant_2016} plus granularity changes at different stages.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\fcolorbox{blue!50!black}{blue!7}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.96\linewidth}\vspace{2pt}
\textbf{\color{blue!50!black}What can we expect from the reform?}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{3pt}
  \item \textbf{Production shifts to the granular side of the market.} The shift is larger for renewables and at ID15 into the IDAs, since IDAs are nearer to delivery. The shift to the IDAs at ID15 is larger when post-DA volatility shocks are higher.
  \item \textbf{Prices.} If the residual demand faced by price-setting firms becomes more inelastic at the quarter-hour (thinner market), prices increase. If it becomes more elastic, prices fall. But it is hour dependent!
  \item \textbf{Richer bid-curve ladders.} Granularity allows more precise bidding, with smaller steps and more price variation, because firms can adjust each quarter rather than the full hour.
  \item \textbf{Market power} effect is ambiguous a priori: more strategic opportunities, but also higher competition. The rationale of the reform was to benefit renewables in the balancing market, and they bid at marginal cost.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{2pt}\end{minipage}}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Data architecture}
I build from scratch a \textbf{full automated data pipeline} for the public market data, using three sources:

\medskip
\fcolorbox{blue!50!black}{blue!7}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.96\linewidth}\vspace{2pt}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{4pt}
  \item \textbf{\color{green!45!black}Wholesale market operator (OMIE).} DA and IDA prices, auction quantities, bilaterals, accepted bid-curves. Continuous market included too, but not the focus.
  \item \textbf{\color{cyan!50!black}System operator (REE) via their API.} Balancing market prices, ancillary services, settlement detail.
  \item \textbf{\color{violet!60!black}European data portal (ENTSO-E) via API.} Cross-border flows, realized wind and solar generation.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{2pt}\end{minipage}}

\bigskip
\textbf{Magnitude.} 420 GB of raw data, 200 GB processed in \textit{parquet} format. Around 10 billion rows. Dedicated external SSD for storage and processing.

\bigskip
\textbf{Time window.} January 2018 -- today, idempotent code. Different granularities across time, changes of hour, MW vs MWh, etc.

\end{frame}

\medskip
\footnotesize
Not all tranches of the bid curves are equally relevant for strategic bidding, we focus on \textbf{tranches around clearing price in that period}, using a bandwidth:
\begin{equation*}
\mathcal{B}_{u,d,p} = \{k: |p_k - \text{MCP}_{d,p}| \leq \delta \}.
\end{equation*}
choosing the bandwidth $\delta = p_{90} - p_{50}$ in the time-window of interest. Note that both the level and the width of $\mathcal{B}_{u,d,p}$ vary across time!
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.50\textwidth}

\fcolorbox{blue!50!black}{blue!7}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.94\linewidth}\vspace{2pt}
\textbf{\color{blue!50!black}Bid-curve shape measures:}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{8pt}\setlength{\parskip}{2pt}
  \item \textbf{Slope} and level of the bid curve: for each curve, fit $p_k = \hat{\alpha} + \hat{\beta}\, Q_k$ in $k\in\mathcal{B}$ and get $(\hat{\alpha}, \hat{\beta})$. Similar with the 2nd-order polynomial to get the \textbf{curvature} $\tilde{\gamma}$.
  \item Robust measures to \textbf{few tranches} inside the band:
  \begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{6pt}\setlength{\parskip}{2pt}
    \item Quantity-weighted Herfindahl over tranches: $\text{HHI}_p = \sum_{k\in\mathcal{B}} w_k^2$, with $w_k = q_k / \sum_{j\in\mathcal{B}} q_j$.
    \item Quantity-weighted SD of tranche prices: $\sigma_p = \sqrt{\sum_{k\in\mathcal{B}} w_k (p_k - \bar p)^2}$, where $\bar p = \sum_{k\in\mathcal{B}} w_k\, p_k$.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\vspace{2pt}\end{minipage}}

\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Residual demand and hour classification}

\begin{center}
\fcolorbox{blue!50!black}{blue!7}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.96\linewidth}\vspace{2pt}
\textbf{\color{blue!50!black}Key idea.} Granularity is useful when there is variation in the residual demand for each firm, and particularly for the strategic bidders. Renewable producers exploit granularity due to operational reasons (forecast updates), since they bid their quantity at marginal cost, $\approx 0$.
\vspace{2pt}\end{minipage}}
\end{center}

\medskip
We calculate the residual demand as the \textbf{thermal gap}: final program net of realized wind and solar generation, $\text{RD}(q,h,d) = [\text{load}-\text{wind}-\text{solar}]_{q,h,d}$, observed at quarter-hour resolution since 2024. To make the comparison scale-free we use the \textbf{coefficient of variation} for each hour of the day, $\mathrm{CV}(h)=\mathbb{E}_d[\text{sd}_q(\text{RD}(q,h,d))]/\mathbb{E}_d[\overline{\text{RD}}(h,d)]$, and split three classes:

\medskip
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{4pt}
\item $\left.\begin{array}{@{}l@{}}
\textbf{Morning ramp } \mathcal{C}_M\text{: } \mathrm{CV}\approx 6.0\%\ (4.0\times\text{ flat}) \\[2pt]
\textbf{Evening ramp } \mathcal{C}_E\text{: } \mathrm{CV}\approx 3.8\%\ (2.5\times\text{ flat})
\end{array}\right\}\ \textbf{Critical }\mathcal{C}=\mathcal{C}_M\cup\mathcal{C}_E,\ \overline{\mathrm{CV}}\approx 4.6\%\ (3.1\times\text{ flat})$
\item \textbf{Flat} $\mathcal{F}$, deep night hours, $\mathrm{CV}\approx 1.5\%$ (baseline).
\item \textbf{Midday} $\mathcal{M}$, $\mathrm{CV}\approx 5.7\%$ ($3.8\times$ flat).
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Identification strategy: bid-curve shape}

\begin{center}
\fcolorbox{blue!50!black}{blue!7}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.96\linewidth}\vspace{4pt}
\textbf{Identification for bid-curve shape.} Check evolution of bid-curve shape measures in \textcolor{blue}{critical vs flat hours, pre vs post reform}, to measure the effect of granularity on bid shape.

\medskip
\textbf{Why use a DiD here?} It differences out across-hour changes in bidding complexity, such as more advanced software, prediction models, etc.
\vspace{4pt}\end{minipage}}
\end{center}

\bigskip
\textbf{\textcolor{red}{Caveat: flat hours are not a perfect controls.}} They are also affected by the reform, but \textbf{economic theory} tells us that the biggest effects of the reform are in critical hours $\to$ \textbf{lower bound of the effect.}

\bigskip
The identification assumption is the usual \textbf{parallel trends:} absence granularity reform, the evolution of the bid shape of critical and flat hours would have been the same.

\bigskip
Note that our \textbf{bid-curve shape measures are robust to seasonal level changes!} Main threat: post-reform shocks that affect differently the shape, not level, to critical or flat hours.

\end{frame}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{frame}{Identification strategy: level variables}
  For level variables like prices, quantities, etc, \textbf{seasonality} is the big issue, since changes in renewable production affect differently critical and flat hours across the year. Moreover, for quantity variables we have to be very very catious and interpret correctly the \textbf{different programs and REE restrictions}.

  \bigskip
  Following previous work of the EPEX reform \citep{markle_huss_2017} we use \textbf{Bayesian Structural Time Series} \citep{brodersen_2015} and \textbf{standard OLS} exploiting all the rich data and granularity to compare \textcolor{blue}{\textbf{before and after}}, since there is \textcolor{red}{\textbf{no clean counterfactual for level-sensitive variables}}.

  \bigskip
  The most important part here is the choice of windows and controls:

\medskip
\fcolorbox{blue!50!black}{blue!7}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.96\linewidth}\vspace{2pt}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{3pt}
   \item \textbf{\color{blue!50!black}Windows.} Long pre-window from 2022 to capture full seasonality across years. The alternative --- reforzada-constant windows, or excluding periods with different regimes --- would shorten the pre-window and lose seasonality and real renewable growth.
   \item \textbf{\color{blue!50!black}Controls.} Wind and solar production, and gas price (the main drivers of price variation in the Spanish market). We also include year-by-year interactions to capture the growth in renewables, crucial to get the depressing effect of solar on midday prices.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{2pt}\end{minipage}}

\end{frame}

\section{Empirical results}

\begin{frame}{Summary of our results}
\vfill
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{8pt}
  \item \textbf{ID15} delivered the headline gains; \textbf{DA15} added little on top.
  \item Aggregate quantities barely move --- granularity \emph{reorganises bidding within the hour}, not dispatch across hours. Slightly move towards the granular market.
  \item Less unilateral market power for the Big-4 dominant firms at both reforms.
  \item Welfare lands on the \emph{imbalance settlement} (cheaper reserves) for ID15, not on the aggregate imbalance volumes.
  \item Granularity made the market more competitive without moving aggregate quantities.
\end{itemize}
\vfill
\centering
\textcolor{red!70!black}{\textbf{This is all after carefully controlling for renewable penetration.}}
\vfill
\end{frame}

\section{Conclusion}

\begin{frame}{Conclusion}
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{6pt}
  \item \textcolor{red!70!black}{\textbf{Aggregate effects of DA15 are small; ID15 delivered the headline gains.}} Significant changes in bidding behaviour at both reforms, though the bid-shape signals are mixed across techs. Evidence of \textcolor{red!70!black}{\textbf{rising solar cannibalization}} --- already a serious profitability issue.
  \item \textbf{Open question:} did \textcolor{red!70!black}{\textbf{market microstructure affect grid stability?}} \citet{cnmc_blackout_recommendations_2026} writes:
    \begin{center}
    \textit{``The evolution to quarter-hourly trading and settlement has made the appearance of transitions between negative and positive prices increasingly frequent, leading to strong changes in production volumes in certain zones.''}
    \end{center}
    Not contrasted, but consistent with the \textcolor{red!70!black}{\textbf{very cautious DA15 adoption after the blackout}} (France and Germany have delayed; Spain re-entered \emph{re-reforzada}). Solar cannibalization may belong to the same story.
  \item The \textcolor{red!70!black}{\textbf{biggest welfare losses live in the ancillary markets}}, not in the wholesale auctions \citep{brown_reguant_2026,davi_arderius_graf_2026}.
  \item \textcolor{red!70!black}{\textbf{Urgent need to strengthen network security}} to bring down retail prices --- not finer time resolution.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}
