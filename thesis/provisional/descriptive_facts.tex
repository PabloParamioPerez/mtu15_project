\noindent\emph{This file collects descriptive facts that any identification design must rest on: how firms bid across hour-classes (critical vs flat), how pivotal and non-pivotal firms differ, how technologies differ, and the regulatory / RT2 / reforzada context in which the reforms operate. Nothing here is an identification claim. The regression outputs from the current reform-window design are in the sibling file \code{thesis/provisional/regression_results.tex}.}

\medskip
\noindent\emph{\color{seasoncol}A note on seasonality.} Throughout this document, where a table reports a per-regime value, the bracketed {\color{seasoncol}colored} number alongside it is the seasonality-adjusted value: the regime's predicted level at \emph{average day-of-year}, from a Frisch-Waugh-Lovell regression of the outcome on regime dummies plus a $K{=}4$ Fourier basis on day-of-year (Newey-West HAC standard errors), pooled across 2022-2026. Fourier-deseasonalization is the standard approach in the electricity time-series literature (Weron 2014, Janczura-Trück-Weron-Wolff 2013); month/year fixed effects are not identified here given non-overlapping regime windows (\S\ref{sec:bid_internal:calendar_discipline}). \textbf{Where the raw and adjusted values move in the same direction across regimes, the documented patterns survive seasonality control.} Where they diverge (large bracketed-vs-raw gaps), the raw cross-regime reading is mostly a calendar-mix artifact and only the adjusted values are meaningfully comparable across regimes.

\subsection*{Calendar coverage and same-calendar baselines}\label{sec:bid_internal:calendar_discipline}
% =====================================================================
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Calendar coverage and same-calendar baselines}

\noindent \textbf{Why this matters.} Spanish electricity is strongly seasonal: heating-driven demand peaks in winter, cooling in summer, hydro inflows peak in spring, solar production peaks at the summer solstice, wind blows hardest in winter. The five reform windows above do \emph{not} span the calendar uniformly --- each window covers a specific subset of calendar months, and these subsets differ across windows. Any cross-regime comparison that does not hold the calendar fixed therefore confounds the regime change with the seasonal pattern. \textbf{This applies to every variable in this document}: bid prices, $D_w$, $\Delta p$ / $\Delta q$, redispatch volumes, imbalance MWh, system-cost levels, RES capture rates. A reader who picks any table in this document should first read Table~\ref{tab:calendar_coverage} below to know which calendar months that regime covers and what comparator is available.

\noindent\textbf{Three categories.} Each regime falls into one of three buckets for the purpose of cross-regime reading:

\begin{enumerate}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
\item \textbf{Clean same-calendar placebo available.} \emph{3-sess, ISP15-win, MTU15-IDA pre-blk.} For each of these, we can restrict the pre-IDA baseline to the same calendar months in 2018--2024 (and average across years), and the difference (regime $-$ pre-IDA same-cal) is interpretable as the regime effect with seasonality differenced out. Any cross-regime reading that uses these windows defaults to this same-calendar comparison unless explicitly stated otherwise.

\item \textbf{Reforzada-confounded, partial calendar-match available.} \emph{MTU15-IDA post-blk.} The window spans May--Sep 2025, which has clean same-calendar comparators in pre-IDA (May--Sep 2018--2023). But these comparators are pre-reforzada. So the difference (MTU15-IDA post-blk $-$ pre-IDA same-cal) confounds (a) the granularity reform, (b) operación reforzada, and (c) the post-blackout system state. There is no calendar period in the data in which reforzada is active but MTU15 is not, so reforzada cannot be differenced out via a same-calendar pre-reform window. For descriptive purposes, May--Sep 2025 is best read as ``post-MTU15-IDA $+$ reforzada $+$ post-blackout, jointly''; see \S\ref{sec:prov:phf_minus_pdbf} on disentangling these channels.

\item \textbf{No clean calendar match.} \emph{DA15/ID15.} The window covers Oct 2025 onward. The same calendar months one year earlier (Oct 2024 -- May 2025) are themselves a mix of pre-MTU15-IDA, ISP15-win, MTU15-IDA pre-blk, and MTU15-IDA post-blk regimes --- so calendar-matching to ``one year ago'' does not produce a single-regime comparator. The cleanest available calendar comparators for DA15/ID15 are the same months in pre-IDA years (Oct 2023 -- May 2024 was largely pre-IDA, with the IDA-3 reform starting only mid-Jun 2024; Oct 2022 -- May 2023 and earlier are all pre-IDA). But even with pre-IDA same-calendar baselines, the reforzada overlay is unresolved by this comparator: DA15/ID15 is the union of MTU15-DA, MTU15-IDA, and reforzada in the same calendar window, and our data does not contain a period where any one of these three is active without the others. Therefore any DA15/ID15 reading should be flagged as descriptive only, with the three confounds explicitly named.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{Discipline applied throughout this document.}

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
\item Per-regime descriptive tables typically report the regime's level (mean per regime); for the regimes in bucket 1, we also report the same-calendar pre-IDA mean wherever the sample size permits, so the reader can read (regime $-$ same-cal pre-IDA) directly. Examples: $D_w$ table (\S\ref{sec:prov:dw_tables}), zonal-dominance bid table (\S\ref{sec:prov:geo:bid_by_dominance}), apuntamiento (\S\ref{sec:prov:apuntamiento}).
\item For bucket-2 windows (MTU15-IDA post-blk), the descriptive reading is explicitly the joint effect of granularity $+$ reforzada $+$ post-blackout. We do not attempt to attribute the difference to any single channel without an out-of-data identification step.
\item For bucket-3 (DA15/ID15), the descriptive reading combines all three reforms with reforzada. Wherever a DA15/ID15 number is reported, we name the confounds.
\item The functional-PCA exercise in \S\ref{sec:prov:fpca} uses pairwise reform-window comparisons with calendar-shifted placebo years (2022, 2023) to explicitly difference out year-on-year seasonality within each placebo. That is the cleanest seasonality-control implementation in the document; we treat it as the benchmark, not the rule.
\item Wherever a table in this document does not report a same-calendar baseline, the reader should consult Table~\ref{tab:calendar_coverage} to identify the regime's calendar window and bucket, and read the absolute level with the seasonal-mix caveat in mind.
\end{itemize}

\section{Hour-class taxonomy: critical, flat, midday}\label{sec:prov:hour_class}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Empirical classification: within-hour standard deviation of residual demand.}
The crit/flat/midday split is \textbf{not assumed} on demand-level grounds (``critical hours are the ones with the highest demand''). It is derived from the within-hour \emph{variability} of residual demand. The intuition is mechanical: \emph{MTU15 granularity has economic content only where the within-hour profile actually moves}. If residual demand is essentially flat within a clock-hour, the hourly clearing price is already a good summary; granular per-quarter pricing has no signal to add. If residual demand changes sharply within the hour (morning ramp, evening peak, solar fade), the hourly clearing price was a compromise across rapidly-changing within-hour conditions; granular pricing unlocks the ability to discriminate across quarters and is therefore where strategic bidding can have a quarter-specific footprint.

Concretely, for clock-hour $h$ on date $d$, let $D_{h,q,d}$ be the residual demand (= load $-$ wind $-$ solar) at quarter $q \in \{1,2,3,4\}$ of that hour. The hour's quarter-mean is $\bar D_{h,d} = \tfrac{1}{4}\sum_q D_{h,q,d}$. We compute, per (date, hour), the within-hour standard deviation
\[
  \sigma_{\text{within}}(h, d) \;=\; \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{4}\sum_{q=1}^{4}\bigl(D_{h,q,d} - \bar D_{h,d}\bigr)^2},
\]
and take the expectation over all 2025 dates to get a clock-hour metric
\[
  \sigma_{\text{within}}(h) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_d\bigl[\sigma_{\text{within}}(h, d)\bigr] \quad [\text{MW}].
\]
The 24-hour ranking of $\sigma_{\text{within}}(h)$ is the actual driver of the classification. Hours with high $\sigma_{\text{within}}$ are critical; hours with low $\sigma_{\text{within}}$ are flat; midday hours are excluded for a separate reason (below). In economic terms, high $\sigma_{\text{within}}$ means the within-hour residual-demand curve moves enough that equilibrium quarter prices and quantities should differ; hour-level pricing was a binding pooling constraint, and granular pricing relaxes it. Strategic firms with within-hour market power therefore have scope to exploit it in critical hours and essentially no scope in flat hours --- which is the heterogeneity Part A's CCGT bid-shape analysis is set up to detect.

\paragraph{Data, window, and frequency.}
$D_{h,q,d}$ is computed from two ENTSO-E Transparency Platform sources, both at 15-minute ISP resolution:
\begin{itemize}
\item Spanish system load: ENTSO-E A65 (\code{load_actual_all.parquet}), filtered to MTU = 15 minutes.
\item Spanish wind + solar generation: ENTSO-E A75 (\code{wind_solar_actual_all.parquet}), PSR types B16 (solar) + B18 (wind offshore) + B19 (wind onshore), filtered to MTU = 15 minutes.
\end{itemize}
Residual demand is the per-ISP arithmetic difference $D = \text{load} - \text{solar} - \text{wind}$.

\emph{Window.} The classifier runs on \textbf{2023-01-01 through 2026-05-14 ($\approx 3.4$ years)}, which is the largest window for which both A65 and A75 are uniformly available at 15-min granularity. ENTSO-E reports Spanish A65/A75 at 60-min before 2022 and at mixed 60/15-min during 2022 (transition to ISP15 was 2024-11-13 in the regulatory calendar but ENTSO-E's data-side switch for Spain happened earlier in 2022), so pre-2023 the within-hour variance of residual demand is undefined --- there is one observation per clock-hour rather than four. We use the longest clean 15-min window because the classifier is a structural feature of the within-day pattern, not a regime-specific statistic; averaging over more years gives a more stable hour-of-day ranking.

\emph{Why ENTSO-E and not ESIOS, given ESIOS has 5-min for demand.} ESIOS (REE) does publish demand at 5-minute native resolution (\code{demand_real} id 1293, available back to 2018) and our ingestion catalog stores it downsampled to 15-min ISP. However, ESIOS' curated wind/solar generation indicators are forecasts (\code{wind_forecast_pen} id 541, \code{solar_pv_forecast} id 542, \code{solar_forecast_combined} id 10034), not actuals, and they are 15-min only from 2022 onward (60-min before). For actual wind/solar generation, ENTSO-E A75 is the only consistent source and binds the window at 2023-01-01. Even if 5-min demand were used on the demand side, the market clears at 15-min ISP and bids are quoted at 15-min ISP, so within-hour variability at 5-min resolution would pick up sub-ISP fluctuations that the market cannot price-discriminate against by construction --- the economic content of granularity, which is what $\sigma_{\text{within}}$ is meant to proxy for, lives at 15-min.

\emph{Day-type pool.} All dates 2023--2026 are pooled (weekday + weekend), consistent with the bid-side analysis in Part A. The weekday-only partition produces the same class assignments (no rank swaps); weekend $\sigma_{\text{within}}$ is uniformly smaller in absolute terms but the morning-ramp / evening-peak pattern survives.

\paragraph{Classification rule.}
The partition is read off the table by contiguous-block structure: \textbf{critical} = the morning-ramp block (5h--8h) + the evening-peak block (16h--22h), 11 hours all with $\sigma_{\text{within}} \in [522, 1303]$ MW; \textbf{flat} = the overnight block (1h--3h), 3 hours all with $\sigma_{\text{within}} \in [336, 357]$ MW; \textbf{midday} = the solar-peak block (11h--14h), 4 hours with $\sigma_{\text{within}} \in [468, 502]$ MW \emph{and} residual demand $< 9$ GW. The 6 remaining hours (0, 4, 9, 10, 15, 23, $\sigma_{\text{within}} \in [412, 753]$ MW) straddle the thresholds and are dropped. No clustering or supervised threshold tuning is involved --- the partition can be reconstructed from the sorted table by visual inspection.

\paragraph{Why we distinguish critical from flat.}
Critical-hour $\sigma_{\text{within}}$ is roughly $1.5$--$4\times$ that of flat hours (522--1,303 MW vs 336--357 MW), reflecting fast within-hour shifts in residual demand: morning demand ramp + solar rise at 5h--9h, solar fade + evening peak at 16h--22h. Granular per-quarter pricing in critical hours can in principle discriminate prices across quarters that face very different supply/demand conditions, so it is where strategic bidding has scope. Flat hours have nearly-flat residual demand within the clock-hour and granular pricing has no economic content to extract --- the four quarter prices, absent friction, should be essentially the same. This is why flat is the within-day control: the (crit $-$ flat) differential strips out any day-level shock (gas, weather, regulation) that affects both hour-classes proportionally, while flat itself carries no granularity-exploitation signal.

\emph{Falsifier already documented in the main paper.} \code{paper.tex} \S2.4: CCGT price-setting share is 9.1\% critical vs 9.0\% flat (almost identical), so TTF/gas-cost shocks cannot propagate asymmetrically across the two hour-classes. The within-day differencing therefore identifies behaviour, not cost shocks.

\emph{Outcome-side validation: post-MTU15-DA actually price-discriminates more in critical hours.} The classifier is a structural proxy: high $\sigma_{\text{within}}$ should predict where granular pricing delivers measurable price dispersion. Post-MTU15-DA (2025-10-01 to 2026-05-14, the only window with 15-min DA quarter prices), the within-hour DA quarter clearing-price standard deviation averages \textbf{6.58 EUR/MWh in critical hours vs 2.13 in flat hours (3.1$\times$ ratio)} --- midday is even tighter (1.51 EUR/MWh, solar-marginal regime). The classifier correctly identifies the hours where post-reform quarter prices actually diverge.

\paragraph{Why we exclude midday from the control.}
Midday hours (11--14) have $\sigma_{\text{within}} \in [468, 502]$ MW, intermediate between critical and flat. By the variability criterion alone, midday is neither clearly ``treated'' (granularity has some scope) nor clearly ``control'' (granularity has zero scope). What pushes midday into a third bucket is the \emph{level} of residual demand and the implied marginal-pricing technology: midday solar peak drives residual demand down to 8.5--8.9 GW (vs $\sim 15$--$23$ GW outside midday), and the marginal price-setting technology shifts from CCGT/hydro to solar PV + storage. Pooling midday into flat as a control would absorb both (a) the strategic-bidding signal we want and (b) the operational solar-vs-thermal differential (a separate channel that has its own dynamics). We exclude midday from the control set entirely. It appears in some descriptive overlays (channel-level histograms of $\Delta p$, $\Delta q$ show crit/midday/flat side by side for comparison), but it is never used as control in the DDD.

\paragraph{Why a 6-hour transition gap.}
Hours 0, 4, 9, 10, 15, 23 sit at the boundaries between regimes. Their $\sigma_{\text{within}}$ values are 412, 587, 753, 499, 677, 510 MW --- straddling the flat/critical thresholds. Rather than force a binary classification, we drop them. The 14-hour analytic sample (11 crit + 3 flat) is the analytic universe for every table in Part A; the 4 midday hours are reserved for descriptive overlay only.

\section{Who price-sets}\label{sec:prov:pricesetter}

\subsection{For the day-ahead market}\label{sec:prov:pricesetter_da}

\paragraph{Method (compressed).} OMIE submits stepwise orders to EUPHEMIA: in-the-money fully accepted, out-of-the-money fully rejected, \textbf{at-the-money can be curtailed}. The price-setter is the unit whose at-MCP step is partially accepted. Operationally, with $q_{\text{below}}$ = sum of unit's bids strictly below MCP, $q_{\text{at}}$ = sum at MCP ($\pm 0.01$ EUR/MWh), and $q_{\text{assigned}}$ = unit's PDBC cleared MWh:
\[
  q_{\text{below}} + \varepsilon \;<\; q_{\text{assigned}} \;<\; q_{\text{below}} + q_{\text{at}} - \varepsilon
\]
Excluded: full acceptance ($q_{\text{assigned}} \ge q_{\text{below}} + q_{\text{at}}$, inframarginal at MCP) and curtailed-to-zero ($q_{\text{assigned}} \le q_{\text{below}}$, often MIC deactivation). \section{Setup: bid-shape metrics ($D_w$, $\Delta p$, $\Delta q$)}\label{sec:prov:dw_decomp}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{$D_w$, in full.}
Let cell $(f, u, d, h)$ index a firm $f$, unit $u$, date $d$, clock-hour $h$. The hour partitions into 4 quarters $q \in \{1,2,3,4\}$. In quarter $q$ the unit submits a multiset of price-quantity sell tranches with $p\in[\underline p, \overline p] \subset \mathbb{R}$ (Spanish SDAC: $\underline p = -500$, $\overline p = 4{,}000$ EUR/MWh; negative bids are admissible) and $x>0$ (MWh, energy for the quarter-slot). Let $c_{d,h}$ be the hour-mean clearing price (justified below) and fix a strategic half-band of half-width $h = 50$ EUR/MWh (justified below) around it. \textbf{We work throughout with band-restricted supply curves}: $N_q$ is the number of \emph{distinct} prices submitted in quarter $q$ \emph{within} $[c-h, c+h]$, and $x_{qk}$ is the sum of quantities at $p_{qk}$. The band-local quarter-$q$ supply curve is the right-continuous step function
\[
  S_q(p) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{N_q} x_{qk}\,\mathbf{1}\{p_{qk}\le p\}, \qquad p \in [c-h, c+h],
\]
with $S_q(c-h) = 0$ and $S_q(c+h) = M_q$ (the band-mass of quarter $q$ --- the total MWh offered \emph{anywhere inside} the band $[c-h, c+h]$, not the offer at the maximum-price threshold). Infra-marginal mass below $c-h$ is excluded by construction: $D_w$ measures the within-band \emph{shape} of supply across quarters, not infra-marginal level offsets.

For two quarters $i, j$, the 1-D Wasserstein-1 distance is the $L^1$ area between cumulative curves, equivalently the $L^1$ distance between (extended) quantile functions:
\[
  W^{\text{band}}_1(S_i, S_j) \;=\; \int_{c-h}^{c+h}\!\big|S_i(p)-S_j(p)\big|\,dp \;=\; \int_{0}^{\max(M_i, M_j)}\!\big|S_i^{-1}(Q)-S_j^{-1}(Q)\big|\,dQ,
\]
where $S_q^{-1}(Q) = c+h$ for $Q > M_q$ pads the smaller curve to common total mass (Vallender, 1973). To isolate variation near the clearing price, weight by an (unnormalized) Epanechnikov-shape kernel:
\[
  K_h(p-c) \;=\; \max\!\left\{0,\;1 - \!\left(\frac{p-c}{h}\right)^{\!2}\right\}, \quad K_h \in [0,1], \quad K_h(c)=1,
\]
\[
  D^{(i,j)}_w \;=\; \int_{c-h}^{c+h}\! K_h(p-c)\,\big|S_i(p)-S_j(p)\big|\,dp \;\le\; W^{\text{band}}_1.
\]
$D_w$ has units EUR (quantity in MWh times price in EUR/MWh).

\paragraph{Why $c$ = hour-mean clearing price.}
The band $[c-h, c+h]$ is meant to isolate the strategic pricing region --- the price range where the unit's bids actually had a non-trivial probability of clearing in this hour. We choose $c = c_{d,h}$ = the hour-mean of the four within-hour quarter clearing prices. Three reasons:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Symmetric across quarters.} The metric is comparing the 4 quarter bid curves; if we used each quarter's \emph{own} clearing price as its own reference, the band would shift across quarters and the within-quarter dissimilarity statistic would no longer be comparable across quarters of the same hour --- we would be measuring shifts of the reference, not shifts of the curve.
\item \textbf{In the relevant pricing region.} The hour-mean sits, by construction, in the middle of the within-hour clearing-price range, so $[c-h, c+h]$ contains all quarter clearings (typically) plus the surrounding band where firms are competing on margin.
\item \textbf{One reference per (cell, hour).} A single $c$ per (date, clock-hour) cell keeps the statistic $D_w^{(i,j)}$ defined on a common interval for all 6 pairs in the cell.
\end{itemize}
\emph{Alternative for robustness:} use the hourly DA clearing price (which is exogenous to within-hour quarter shaping by construction --- the DA clears at the hour level even post-MTU15-IDA). This would be more exogenous to the firm's own shaping but would lose connection to actually realised within-hour prices. Flagged for sensitivity.

\paragraph{Why $h = 50$ EUR/MWh.}
The half-width $h$ is the tuning parameter that controls how much of the bid curve enters the dissimilarity statistic. We pick $h = 50$ EUR/MWh on three grounds:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Order-of-magnitude of clearing prices.} Spanish DA clearing prices in 2024--25 typically sit in the 50--150 EUR/MWh range; a $\pm 50$ band captures bid mass within roughly one ``clearing-price width'' of clearing, which is the region where bid placement matters for being on the margin.
\item \textbf{CCGT marginal-cost scale.} CCGT marginal costs in our sample range roughly 80--200 EUR/MWh; $h = 50$ is well below this range, so the band excludes far-from-clearing bids that effectively never compete.
\item \textbf{SDAC range scale.} On a $[-500, 4000]$ price domain $h = 50$ is a small slice ($\sim 1\%$ of the upper bound) --- it does \emph{not} include strategic high-cap bids (which sit at 800--3000 EUR/MWh and play a different role).
\end{itemize}
\emph{This is a choice that should be tested empirically, not asserted.} The check below sweeps $h \in \{20, 50, 100, 200\}$ and confirms the qualitative ranking (GN dominant on $\Delta q$, GE leaning on $\Delta p$, IB/HC negligible, flat hours $\approx 0$) holds at all four values. A more principled empirical anchor for a future pass would be to pick $h$ as the price interval that captures, say, 90\% of clearing-price-setter bids for the median CCGT unit, computed from the bid-side data; we use $h=50$ as the working choice and treat it as a tuning parameter, not a structural one.

\textit{Reading the sensitivity.} Across all four $h$:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{GN's $\Delta q$ dominance is robust.} GN critical-hour $\Delta q$-active rises monotonically with $h$ (12.5\% $\to$ 18.4\% $\to$ 24.1\% $\to$ 24.5\%) because a wider band captures more pairs that differ in band-mass. The \emph{ranking} (GN $\gg$ everyone else on $\Delta q$ critical) is preserved everywhere.
\item \textbf{GE's $\Delta p$ leaning is robust} and saturates by $h=50$ (0.69 $\to$ 1.56 $\to$ 1.64 $\to$ 1.64\%). The price-channel signal is concentrated within $\pm 50$ EUR/MWh of clearing; expanding the band further captures essentially no additional $\Delta p$-active cells.
\item \textbf{Flat hours stay $\approx 0$} across all firms and all $h$ on both channels (max value: IB flat $\Delta q$-active at $h=200$ is 2.5\%; everything else $\le 1.4\%$). The crit-vs-flat asymmetry is the same first-order story under every band choice.
\item \textbf{IB shows the steepest scaling with $h$} on both channels, suggesting IB's near-clearing bidding is sparser and only registers as ``active'' once the band is large enough to catch its quarter-pair differences. Not a robustness concern (IB is small in absolute terms) but worth noting: IB's signal is at the edge of detectability.
\end{itemize}
The bolded $h=50$ column is the working choice for the headline tables that follow.

\paragraph{Cell-level aggregation: max vs mean across the 6 within-hour pairs.}
A cell has 4 within-hour quarters and therefore $\binom{4}{2} = 6$ pairwise dissimilarities $D_w^{(i,j)}$. We aggregate to a single cell-level statistic by taking the \emph{maximum}:
\[
  D^{\text{cell}}_w \;=\; \max_{1\le i<j\le 4}\,D^{(i,j)}_w.
\]
The choice between max and mean matters in two different ways:
\begin{itemize}
\item For the \emph{binary flag} $D^{\text{cell}}_w > 0$ (the metric in the operational-vs-strategic table below, in the IDA pre/post-blackout table, and in the $\Delta p$/$\Delta q$-active shares in Table~\ref{tab:dw_decomp}), the max-vs-mean choice is \textbf{irrelevant}: a cell has any pair $> 0$ if and only if max $> 0$ if and only if mean $> 0$. The flag rates reported in Tables~\ref{tab:prov:operational_strategic_asym}, \ref{tab:ida_pre_post}, \ref{tab:dw_decomp} therefore do not depend on this choice.
\item For the \emph{conditional intensity} (e.g.\ the median $D_w$ rows in Table~\ref{tab:dw_mass_median}), the choice does matter: max picks the most-different pair and biases toward the upper tail of pair-wise dissimilarities; mean averages over all 6 pairs and dampens outlier-pair effects. We report max because the substantive question is ``did any within-hour shaping occur in this cell, and if so, how large was the most-different pair?''; a mean-aggregated companion (``how large is the typical pair?'') is a worthwhile robustness and is flagged for the next pass.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Decomposition into price and quantity channels.}
We split the within-band variation into a price channel and a quantity channel:
\[
  \Delta q_{ij} \;=\; |M_i - M_j|\;\;[\text{MWh}],
  \qquad
  \Delta p_{ij} \;=\; \frac{1}{\min(M_i, M_j)}\int_{0}^{\min(M_i, M_j)}\!\!\big|S_i^{-1}(Q) - S_j^{-1}(Q)\big|\,dQ\;\;[\text{EUR/MWh}].
\]
$\Delta p$ captures the mean price shift on the common-mass portion --- a price-only adjustment with band-mass totals held fixed, in EUR/MWh. $\Delta q$ captures band-mass mismatch --- a quantity-only adjustment with prices on the common-mass portion held fixed, in MWh. Cell-level $\Delta p$, $\Delta q$ are the max over the 6 pairs (same caveats as above).

\paragraph{Caveat on $\Delta q$: this number is coarse on its own.}
$\Delta q_{ij} = |M_i - M_j|$ is the \emph{absolute} difference between the two quarters' total band-mass (in MWh). The choice is dictated by the Wasserstein upper-bound proof below --- without exactly this $\Delta q$, the bound $W^{\text{band}}_1 \le \Delta p_{ij}\,M_i + 2h\,\Delta q_{ij}$ does not hold and the decomposition is not consistent. But \textbf{on its own, raw $\Delta q$ is uninformative without context}: 10 MWh of asymmetric mass between quarters is huge in a 20 MWh band-mass cell and negligible in a 500 MWh one. The tables below currently report $\Delta q$ in raw MWh; a relative version $\Delta q / \bar M$ where $\bar M = (M_i + M_j)/2$ is the mean band-mass would scale-normalise it, and is flagged for the next pass. In the empirical reading below we lean on the \emph{active/inactive} distinction ($\Delta q > 0$ vs $\Delta q = 0$) and on the loose-bound diagnostic at the end of this section, both of which are robust to the scale issue. (A further weakness: \emph{two quarters with identical total band-mass but different curve shapes get $\Delta q = 0$ even though the quantity channel is non-trivial.} What survives is the price channel $\Delta p$; the decomposition then attributes all shape variation to price, even when it is properly a shape difference in quantity.)

\paragraph{Short proof of consistency.}
WLOG $M_i \le M_j$. Extending $S_i^{-1}(Q) = c+h$ for $Q \in (M_i, M_j]$ gives both quantile functions a common domain $[0, M_j]$ and common total mass $M_j$. The 1-D Wasserstein-1 duality for distributions with common mass (Vallender, 1973) gives
\[
  W^{\text{band}}_1 \;=\; \int_{c-h}^{c+h}\!\big|S_i(p) - S_j(p)\big|\,dp
  \;=\; \int_{0}^{M_j}\!\big|S_i^{-1}(Q) - S_j^{-1}(Q)\big|\,dQ.
\]
Split the right-hand integral at $Q = M_i$:
\begin{align*}
  W^{\text{band}}_1
    &= \underbrace{\int_0^{M_i}\!\big|S_i^{-1}(Q) - S_j^{-1}(Q)\big|\,dQ}_{=\;\Delta p_{ij}\,M_i\;\;\text{(definition of $\Delta p$)}}
    \;+\; \underbrace{\int_{M_i}^{M_j}\!\big(c{+}h - S_j^{-1}(Q)\big)\,dQ}_{=:\,R_{ij}}.
\end{align*}
The integrand of $R_{ij}$ lies in $[0, 2h]$ (since $S_j^{-1}(Q) \in [c-h, c+h]$), so $0 \le R_{ij} \le 2h\,\Delta q_{ij}$. Since $K_h \in [0,1]$ on the band,
\[
  D^{(i,j)}_w \;\le\; W^{\text{band}}_1 \;\le\; \Delta p_{ij}\,M_i + 2h\,\Delta q_{ij}.
\]

\emph{Limiting cases.} (i) If $M_i = M_j$, then $R_{ij}=0$ and $\Delta q_{ij}=0$, so $W^{\text{band}}_1 = \Delta p_{ij}\,M$: all variation is on the price channel. (ii) If $S_i^{-1} \equiv S_j^{-1}$ on $[0, M_i]$ (quantile functions coincide on common mass), then $\Delta p_{ij}=0$ and $W^{\text{band}}_1 = R_{ij} \in [0,\,2h\,\Delta q_{ij}]$: all variation is on the quantity channel. The two channels are orthogonal modes verifiable at the boundary cases. \hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Empirical loose-bound diagnostic.}
The upper bound $W^{\text{band}}_1 \le \Delta p_{ij}\,M_i + 2h\,\Delta q_{ij}$ is mathematically tight only in the two limiting cases above. Empirically in our CCGT data $R_{ij} \ll 2h\,\Delta q_{ij}$ (median $R / (2h\,\Delta q) = 0.003$): the extra band-mass sits near the top of the band, so the $\Delta q$ channel contributes little to $W^{\text{band}}_1$ even when $\Delta q$ is large. The decomposition is mathematically exact in the limit cases but the upper bound is loose by $\sim 13\times$ on average. \textbf{Interpret $\Delta p$ and $\Delta q$ as channel \emph{indicators} (active/inactive, and which channel is dominant), not as additive contributions to $W^{\text{band}}_1$.}

\subsection{Per-regime in-band share (bid concentration near MCP)}\label{sec:prov:steepness}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Definition.} For each (date, period, sell-unit) cell we compute the \emph{share} of the unit's offered MW falling within $\pm 50$ EUR/MWh of the clearing price:
\[
\text{share}_{\text{band}} = \frac{\sum_{|p_{\text{bid}} - p_{\text{clear}}| \le 50} q}{\sum q}
= \frac{\text{MW}_{\text{band}}}{\text{MW}_{\text{total}}}.
\]
The numerator uses the same kernel-band half-width $h$ as $D_w$ (\S\ref{sec:prov:dw_decomp}). The denominator normalises by the unit's total offered MW for that period, isolating the \emph{shape} of bid concentration near MCP from the unit's operational \emph{scale} (which varies with technical commitment --- larger MW online at night $\Rightarrow$ mechanically larger $\text{MW}_{\text{band}}$ even without strategic change). We aggregate per (tech, firm, year-month, hour-class), then take the mean across months within each reform regime.

\textbf{Coverage:} 28.4 M DA per-cell rows $+$ 33.2 M IDA per-cell rows, June 2024 -- January 2026. Per-cell parquet aggregates one row per (date, period, unit) with the in-band MW, total MW, $n$ tranches in band, etc., for both DA (\texttt{cab}/\texttt{det}) and IDA (\texttt{icab}/\texttt{idet}).

\paragraph{Methodological caveats (read before interpreting).}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Seasonality is controlled via Fourier-deseasonalized adjusted values in {\color{seasoncol}brackets}.} Each cell of the bid-shape tables (Tables~\ref{tab:bidshape_da}, \ref{tab:bidshape_ida}) shows the raw mean alongside the predicted share at \emph{average day-of-year} from a Frisch-Waugh-Lovell regression: $\text{logit}(\text{share}_t) = \alpha + \sum_r \beta_r \mathbf{1}\{\text{regime}_t = r\} + \sum_{k=1}^{4} \text{Fourier}_k(t) + \varepsilon_t$, fit on the pooled 2022-2026 daily-(tech, hour-class) panel with Newey-West HAC standard errors ($\text{maxlags}{=}14$). Fourier basis on day-of-year is the standard seasonal smoother in the electricity time-series literature (Weron 2014, Janczura-Trück-Weron-Wolff 2013); month FE are not identified given non-overlapping regime windows (\S\ref{sec:bid_internal:calendar_discipline}).
\item \textbf{Band width $h = 50$ EUR/MWh is a modelling choice.} It matches the kernel band used for $D_w$. A wider band ($h = 100$) captures more of the scarcity-priced CCGT tail; a narrower band ($h = 25$) focuses on the immediately-marginal layer. We report a sensitivity below (Table~\ref{tab:bidshape_da_h_sensitivity}): the qualitative reform-window pattern is robust across $h \in \{25, 50, 100\}$.
\item \textbf{Per-cell share has a denominator-effect floor for techs with very low total bidding.} Units that submit only a single block of MW pre-priced near MCP will have share $\to 1$ trivially. This affects the absolute level of the share for some tech-firm combinations (e.g., GST wind aggregators with single-block strategies, see \S\ref{sec:prov:pricesetter_da}) but does not invalidate cross-regime comparisons within a given (tech, firm).
\item \textbf{The fPCA in \S\ref{sec:prov:fpca} is the cleaner version of the same exercise}: it works in quantile space (naturally normalised, monotone-preserving) and runs regressions with seasonal controls. Read the share tables here as a quick orientation; the fPCA is the methodologically tighter complement.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Functional PCA of bid-curve shape changes across regimes}\label{sec:prov:fpca}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Motivation.} The scalar bid-shape metrics in §\ref{sec:prov:steepness} (MW in band, $n$ tranches, $\Delta p$, $\Delta q$, $D_w$) are interpretable individually but ad-hoc collectively. A principled alternative: treat each per-(unit, date, period) sell bid stack as a \emph{functional observation} --- the supply curve $S(p)$ --- and let the data tell us which dimensions of curve-shape variation matter most. Functional PCA (fPCA) extracts orthogonal modes of variation. Each curve is then summarised by a small set of scalar scores $\xi_{ik}$, one per principal component, and the change in curve shape across regimes can be quantified by regressing scores on regime indicators (with seasonality controls).

\paragraph{Brief on fPCA.} View each per-(unit, date, period) bid curve as an element $f_i \in L^2([0,1])$ with $f_i(q) = S_i^{-1}(q)$, and let $\bar f$ be the sample mean function. fPCA finds an orthonormal basis $\{\phi_k\}_{k\ge 1} \subset L^2$ such that the scalar projections $\xi_{ik} = \langle f_i - \bar f,\, \phi_k\rangle_{L^2}$ are uncorrelated across $k$ and the first $K$ of them capture as much sample variance as possible. Equivalently, the $\phi_k$ are the eigenfunctions of the empirical covariance operator $\hat C \phi(s) = \int \hat c(s,t)\, \phi(t)\, dt$ with $\hat c(s,t) = n^{-1} \sum_i (f_i - \bar f)(s)(f_i - \bar f)(t)$. The Karhunen–Lo\`eve expansion $f_i = \bar f + \sum_k \xi_{ik}\, \phi_k$, truncated at small $K$, replaces each curve by a few scalar scores. On a discrete quantile grid this reduces to a singular-value decomposition of the centered $n \times 99$ data matrix; $\phi_k$ is the $k$-th right singular vector and the variance share is the squared singular value normalised by their sum.

\paragraph{Method.} We represent each curve in \emph{quantile} space (price as a function of cumulative MW): $S^{-1}(q)$ on a fixed grid $q \in \{0.01, 0.02, \ldots, 0.99\}$. This is naturally monotone, scale-normalised (each unit's curve spans $[0, $ unit capacity$]$), and matches the geometry used by Portela (2017) for Iberian residual demand curves. Pooled per tech (so that CCGT, hydro, etc. are not mixed), we fit fPCA via singular-value decomposition on a stratified sample of 1{,}000 curves per (regime $\times$ calendar-month) cell. Then we project all per-(unit/firm, date, period) curves onto the basis to obtain scores $\xi_{ik}$ for $k = 1, \ldots, 5$.

\textbf{Granularity}: per-(unit, date, period) for the strategic technologies (CCGT, Hydro, Hydro\_pump, Nuclear). Per-(firm, date, period) for RES (Wind, Solar PV, Cogen, Solar Thermal), where 1000+ small distributed units make per-unit fPCA computationally infeasible (would need 22~GB RAM) and economically meaningless (the firm-level aggregator is the strategic decision-maker).

For each (tech, firm) and each pairwise reform, we restrict the sample to pre $+$ post regimes only and run, separately for each PC $k$:
\begin{align*}
\xi_{i,k} = \alpha &+ \beta_{0,k}\, \mathds{1}[\text{post}_i]
                    + \!\sum_{h \in \{\text{Flat}, \text{Mid}\}}\! \beta_{h,k}\, \mathds{1}[\text{post}_i]\,\mathds{1}[\text{hour}_i = h] \\
                   &+ \!\sum_{m=1}^{3}\! \left(a_{m,k} \sin\!\tfrac{2\pi m\, \text{doy}_i}{365} + b_{m,k} \cos\!\tfrac{2\pi m\, \text{doy}_i}{365}\right) + \gamma_k\, X_i + \varepsilon_{i,k}
\end{align*}
where the Fourier basis on day-of-year (3 harmonics, 6 columns) provides a \emph{parametric seasonal control} on top of calendar-month FE inside $X_i$ --- finite-dimensional and fully parametric; we choose 3 harmonics as a fixed modelling choice, not via a data-driven smoother. The post-effect on Critical hours is $\beta_{0, k}$; on Flat hours $\beta_{0, k} + \beta_{\text{Flat}, k}$; on Midday $\beta_{0, k} + \beta_{\text{Mid}, k}$. Reference: pre regime $\times$ Critical hour. This is the cleanest single-shock identification the data supports per reform.

\paragraph{Reading the pairwise reform evidence.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{ISP15} (3-sess $\to$ ISP15-win): \emph{GE} CCGT curve shifts \emph{down} sharply ($-4{,}116$ on PC1 in Critical hours) --- the Endesa CCGT fleet repriced cheaper after the settlement reform. \emph{GN} CCGT also shifts down ($-1{,}372$ Critical), more mildly in Flat ($-111$). \emph{HC} and \emph{REP} go the opposite direction (small upward shifts). \emph{Hydro}: large divergence --- GN hydro down sharply ($-2{,}675$); GE hydro up ($+442$). The reading: ISP15 induced a strategic repositioning that is firm-specific in direction. The settlement-discipline channel discussed above (\S\ref{sec:prov:full_cascade}) does not require all firms to shift the same way --- it requires the position to be \emph{more accurate}, which can mean different bid-shape responses depending on how each firm was previously over/under-bidding.
\item \textbf{MTU15-IDA} (ISP15-win $\to$ DA60/ID15 pre-blk, 40-day window): \emph{GE} CCGT continues to shift down ($-3{,}513$). \emph{IB} CCGT shifts \emph{up} ($+798$) on Critical and Flat (Iberdrola repositions its DA bid stack higher when MTU15-IDA opens; consistent with the q1-drop / supply-withholding story in §\ref{sec:prov:q1_drop}). \emph{REP} also moves up sharply. \emph{Hydro}: smaller, mixed direction. Small sample caveat: only 40 post-window days for the precision of the pre-blk pre-blackout window.
\item \textbf{MTU15-DA} (post-blk $\to$ DA15/ID15): \emph{IB} CCGT shifts up modestly on Critical ($+313$), down on Flat ($-274$); the cross-hour-class effect reverses sign. \emph{GN} CCGT shifts up everywhere ($+322$ to $+1{,}233$). \emph{HC} CCGT shifts up ($+1{,}470$ Critical). \emph{GE} CCGT keeps drifting down ($-390$). The MTU15-DA reform is the noisiest pairwise comparison --- mixed sign across firms suggests the reform did not deliver a uniform bid-shape change, consistent with the partial-efficiency / Fase-I-rising reading in §\ref{sec:prov:full_cascade}.
\item \textbf{Firm heterogeneity is large.} The fact that GE, GN, IB, HC, REP can move in opposite directions under the same reform means the average ``CCGT response'' is hiding a redistribution. The Big-4 are responding to different incentives or face different operational constraints. For identification, this means the right firm partition (price-setter status, zonal-dominance, vertical-integration) matters more than the all-CCGT mean.
\item \textbf{Seasonal control: matters.} Without the Fourier(doy) basis, the ISP15 pairwise compares Jun--Nov 2024 to Dec 24--Mar 25 --- pure season shift. With the Fourier control, we are netting out the smooth annual cycle; the remaining coefficient is the reform-specific shift. Robustness against a different season-control (cubic spline on doy, calendar-week FE) would be a useful next check.
\item \textbf{What this design cannot identify (outcome-specific flag).} For the MTU15-DA pairwise comparison, MTU15-DA and reforzada are both active in the post-window. The pairwise coefficient describes the joint shift; it does not isolate the MTU15-DA component for any outcome. The 40-day DA60/ID15 pre-blk window is the only sample period that is post-MTU15-IDA and pre-reforzada simultaneously --- the MTU15-IDA pairwise comparison powered by that window is therefore the cleanest single-reform read we have on outcomes covered there. Separating MTU15-DA from reforzada (in a future identification design, not here) will require a geographic or unit-level discontinuity --- non-reforzada-required CCGTs (Norte/Aragón zones) vs reforzada-required CCGTs in the same window.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Caveat on PC interpretation.} The reading above focuses on PC1 because it captures most of the variance. PC2 and PC3 capture shape effects (high-low region trade-off, scarcity tail) that the level-shift interpretation misses. The full curve shifts (Fig above) show the combined effect of all 5 PCs and are the correct visual summary; the per-PC tables are decompositions.

\subsubsection{Descriptive parallel-trends check, DA-side}\label{sec:prov:fpca_placebo}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Motivation.} The Fourier(doy) basis controls for \emph{within-year} seasonality, but not for cross-year shocks (gas-price levels, solar-capacity growth, weather years) that move bid curves independently of the reform. To sharpen the descriptive reading we run the exact same pairwise PC1 specification on the same calendar windows in earlier years, where no Spanish-market reform fired. If the placebo-year coefficients are near zero and the real-year coefficient is large, the descriptive interpretation is much sharper. If placebo-year coefficients are already large, the Fourier control alone is not enough.

This is a descriptive check, not an ATT/ATE estimation. We do not slap a treatment-effect label on the real-vs-placebo difference; we read the columns side-by-side and flag where the real-year coefficient is distinctive.

\paragraph{Scope.} \textbf{DA-side only.} IDA bidding format changed at 2024-06-14 (6 MIBEL regional sessions $\to$ 3 SIDC European sessions), so pre-reform IDA placebo years are not directly comparable. DA bidding format is stable back to 2018, so DA placebos are clean for all three reforms. We use 2022 and 2023 as placebo calendar years, projecting their DA bid curves onto the \emph{existing} fPCA basis (fit on 2024-2026; no refit). Techs covered: CCGT (the headline tech, 86\% PC1 variance), Hydro, Nuclear --- the three with the most variance-dense bid stacks and the strongest economic stake in each reform.

\paragraph{What the 2022 calendar reads.} 2022 was the Russian-gas / Iberian price-cap year. Coefficients tend to be large because the bid stack itself moved more than usual under the gas-price shock. We report 2022 anyway but read it with caution: a large 2022 placebo can reflect macro shocks rather than ``no-reform Dec-Mar drift''. The cleaner placebo is 2023, post-crisis and pre-MTU15 reforms.

\paragraph{Reading the placebo evidence (descriptive).} Compare each ``Real'' column entry against its 2023 placebo (the cleaner reference); 2022 is a noisy macro-shock baseline. Six readings stand out:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{ISP15, GE CCGT effect is \emph{not} reform-specific.} The pairwise table above (Table~\ref{tab:fpca_isp15}) showed GE CCGT critical-hour PC1 of $-4{,}116$ under ISP15 --- the biggest single coefficient in that table. The 2023 placebo gives $-4{,}546$ --- essentially the same. The Dec-Mar window has a persistent downward drift for GE CCGT that the Fourier within-year control does not absorb. The original $-4{,}116$ reading was overstated; the reform-specific component is small after parallel-trends.
\item \textbf{ISP15, REP CCGT effect IS distinctive.} Real $+231$ vs placebos $-340$ (2022) and $-421$ (2023): same magnitude, \emph{opposite sign}. REP's bid stack moves \emph{up} under ISP15 against a clear prior \emph{down} drift --- a reform-specific shift that survives the parallel-trends check.
\item \textbf{MTU15-IDA, IB CCGT effect IS distinctive.} Real $+798$ vs placebos $-161$ (2022) and $+329$ (2023). The IB ``DA-withhold, IDA-recover'' reading from the previous itemize (Iberdrola pulls supply from DA when MTU15-IDA opens) survives the parallel-trends check --- the real shift is more than double the 2023 placebo.
\item \textbf{MTU15-IDA, REP CCGT and GN Hydro effects are distinctive.} REP CCGT $+638$ real vs placebos $-408$ / $-161$ (opposite sign); GN Hydro $+549$ real vs placebos $-544$ / $-1{,}273$ (sign-flip). Both are reform-specific upward repositionings.
\item \textbf{MTU15-DA, HC CCGT effect IS distinctive.} Real $+1{,}470$ vs placebos $-1{,}080$ (2022) and $-896$ (2023): same magnitude, opposite sign. Consistent with the Aragón/Levante CCGT fleet repricing upward when reforzada binds in their zone (HC's CCGTs are mostly inside the reinforced-operation zones).
\item \textbf{MTU15-DA, GN CCGT and GN Hydro effects are distinctive.} GN CCGT real $+322$ vs placebos $-2{,}675$ (energy-crisis) / $+156$ (cleaner) --- real is the largest positive. GN Hydro real $+1{,}871$ vs placebos $+461$ / $-400$ --- real is more than four times larger.
\item \textbf{Nuclear coefficients are unstable across years.} Real, p23, p22 swing across thousands of EUR/MWh for the same nuclear unit at the same firm. Two reasons: (i) Nuclear PC1 captures only $62\%$ of variance vs $86\%$ for CCGT, so the level shift is a weaker summary statistic; (ii) the 40-day MTU15-IDA post-window has very few nuclear cells per firm. We would not lean on nuclear PC1 placebo readings.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Cross-cutting takeaways.}
\begin{itemize}
\item The naive pairwise coefficients (Tables~\ref{tab:fpca_isp15}--\ref{tab:fpca_da15}) overstate some ``reform effects'' (GE CCGT under ISP15) and confirm others (IB CCGT and REP CCGT under MTU15-IDA; HC CCGT under MTU15-DA).
\item The distinction is firm-by-firm, not market-wide. Each reform produces a small set of firm-tech cells where the parallel-trends-augmented reading is sharp; for the rest, the descriptive evidence is weaker than the naive pairwise coefficient suggested.
\item Sign-flips between placebo and real (REP-ISP15, REP-IDA, HC-DA) are the cleanest descriptive signal we have. They show a bid-stack repositioning that is not present in any earlier calendar-shifted window.
\item For the descriptive purpose of this document, the parallel-trends check is meant to flag which firm-tech-reform cells are robust to year-specific drift. It is not a formal identification design; cross-year variation in renewable capacity, gas/CO\textsubscript{2} prices, and demand still affects every cell.
\end{itemize}

\part{Part B --- Regulatory and confounder context}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{The REE program-and-intervention chain (brief primer).} Every delivery day a firm program for each unit-period is built up through a chain of OMIE clearings and REE redispatch steps. Each step writes the next-level program (PDBC $\to$ PDBF $\to$ PDVP $\to$ PHF $\to$ PHFC $\to$ P48); each transition involves either a market clearing or a REE technical-restriction (RT) intervention. The schematic is:

\begin{center}\scriptsize
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
\begin{tabular}{l l l l}
\toprule
Step & New program & Mechanism & Active in our cost cascade as\ldots \\
\midrule
DA clearing (D-1, 12:00) & \textbf{PDBC} & OMIE marginal-price DA cleared MW & DA spot \\
\quad $+$ bilateral contracts & \textbf{PDBF} & OMIE adds BRP-communicated bilaterals & --- \\
\quad $+$ REE \textbf{pre-IDA RT} & \textbf{PDVP} & \emph{Fase I} (pay-as-bid congestion + reserve) $+$ \emph{Fase II} (offer redispatch) & \textbf{Fase I subir/bajar}, \textbf{Fase II subir/bajar} \\
\quad $+$ IDA session $s + $ REE \textbf{post-IDA RT} & \textbf{PHF}$(s)$ & Per-session IDA clear + REE security tweak (``RT2''); 3 sessions in SIDC era & --- (folded into IDA + DA settlement) \\
\quad $+$ continuous round $r + $ REE post-continuous RT & \textbf{PHFC}$(r)$ & 24 rounds/day; per-round continuous + REE tweak & --- (folded into continuous settlement) \\
\quad $+$ real-time RT (delivery, D) & \textbf{P48} & REE \emph{Tiempo Real} (TR) intervention; live updates & \textbf{TR subir/bajar} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The energy for REE's redispatch comes from balancing services activated alongside: \textbf{FCR} (primary, automatic), \textbf{aFRR} (secondary; \emph{capacity} reserved D-1 by auction, \emph{energy} activated continuously via PICASSO/manual), \textbf{mFRR} (tertiary, manual), \textbf{RR} (replacement reserve), \textbf{Gestión de Desvíos} (between mFRR and aFRR for ramp deviations), and \textbf{RPA} (operational reserve). These six services together close the gap between PDBF and physical delivery. Imbalance settlement closes per ISP at the end of day D and is dual-priced (\emph{cobro} vs \emph{pago}). The full cost decomposition --- Fase I, Fase II, TR, aFRR, mFRR, RR, RPA, GD, RES curtailment, imbalance --- is in \S\ref{sec:prov:full_cascade}; the post-IDA RT proxy (PHF $-$ PIBCA) used throughout this Part B is documented in \texttt{docs/notes/SPANISH\_MARKET\_STRUCTURE.md} \S5.5.

\textbf{Two recurring labels in this Part:} (i) ``\emph{Reforzada}'' = REE's continuous post-blackout (2025-04-28 onward) stance of larger pre-IDA and post-IDA RT, primarily Fase I-up, to keep zonal voltage and inertia margins. (ii) ``\emph{RT2}'' (project shorthand) = the post-IDA RT leg specifically, measured as PHF.assigned $-$ PIBCA.assigned at maximum-session rows per (unit, period). Both are confounders for the MTU15-DA pairwise comparison (whose pre- and post-windows are both post-blackout).

\smallskip

\noindent\emph{Reading guide for Part B.} The empirical phenomenon comes first (\S\ref{sec:prov:q1_drop}: the CCGT $q_1$ drop --- the headline post-reform structural break in DA auction-cleared CCGT volume). Then the absorbing mechanism (\S\ref{sec:prov:phf_minus_pdbf}: REE post-clearing CCGT inclusion via the PO 3.2 restrictions cascade). Then the enabling structural feature (\S\ref{sec:prov:geo}: geographic CCGT concentration that gives certain firms pay-as-bid local power on their zones). Then the bid-side response (\S\ref{sec:prov:bid_vs_rt2}: do DA bid prices differ systematically on days with large post-DA RT corrections?). Then the system-cost view (\S\ref{sec:prov:full_cascade}: the full ajuste cascade --- prices, costs, per-day equivalents, and the per-cause-code TR breakdown). Then the RES side-effect (\S\ref{sec:prov:apuntamiento}: solar PV capture rate and cannibalisation under growing capacity). Then the enforcement context (\S\ref{sec:prov:cournot_cnmc}: Cournot reading + CNMC 2019/2023 sanction precedent + 2026 blackout-cohort expedientes). All of these are confounders or structural features that any reform-window identification needs to hold constant.

\subsection{Identification implication}\label{sec:prov:q1_drop:ident}

The Ito--Reguant-style $q_2/q_1$ ratio (where $q_2$ is a counterfactual that includes the unit's full bid stack and $q_1$ is what cleared) is itself endogenous to \emph{operación reforzada}: the unit-day-hours that clear DA in 2025 are a selected sub-sample relative to 2024 (only the heaviest demand days, where even the high bid clears). Same-calendar-month $+$ unit FE help but do not fully address this composition shift. The cleaner descriptive approach is to focus on the \emph{bid side} (the decision to stay out) rather than on the conditional clearing ratio $q_2 / q_1$. The bid-side reading is in \S\ref{sec:prov:bid_vs_rt2}.

\subsection{CCGT marginal cost and the Fase~I scarcity rent}\label{sec:prov:phf_minus_pdbf:mc}

To assess how much of the Fase~I price represents rent above short-run marginal cost (SRMC), we compute a transparent bottom-up MC estimate for the Spanish CCGT fleet using observable fuel and CO\textsubscript{2} inputs.

\textbf{Formula.}
\[
\mathrm{MC}_{\text{CCGT}} = \frac{P_{\text{gas}}}{\eta} + \frac{P_{\text{CO}_2} \cdot \mathrm{EF}}{\eta} + \mathrm{VOM}
\]
where $P_{\text{gas}}$ is the natural-gas spot price (EUR/MWh-thermal, LHV basis), $\eta$ is the thermal-to-electric efficiency, $P_{\text{CO}_2}$ is the EUA spot price, $\mathrm{EF}$ is the gas combustion emission factor (tCO\textsubscript{2}/MWh-thermal), and VOM is variable operations \& maintenance.

\textbf{Parameter sources (deliberately transparent).}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item \textbf{$\eta = 0.55$ (LHV) --- central, not conservative.} Spanish fleet (predominantly 2002--2008 vintage, GE 9F and Siemens SGT5-4000F class) lies in the 52--57\% LHV range. References: IEA \emph{Projected Costs of Generating Electricity} (2020), Table~3.7 (modern CCGT $56$--$62\%$ best-of-class); BNEF LCOE update 2024 (Spanish fleet $\sim 54$\%). \textbf{Important framing}: higher $\eta$ lowers MC (fuel cost per MWh-elec decreases), which raises the computed premium. So $\eta = 0.55$ is the \emph{central estimate}. For a conservative \emph{lower bound} on the premium over MC, use $\eta = 0.52$ (low end of Spanish fleet) --- this raises MC by $\sim 8$ EUR/MWh at typical gas prices and shrinks the headline premium by the same amount.
\item \textbf{$\mathrm{EF} = 0.20$ tCO\textsubscript{2}/MWh-thermal}. Standard natural-gas combustion CO\textsubscript{2} emission factor. Reference: IPCC \emph{2006 Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories}, Volume 2 (Energy), Table~1.4: natural gas $= 56.1$ kgCO\textsubscript{2}/GJ LHV $= 56.1 \times 3.6 / 1{,}000 = 0.202$ tCO\textsubscript{2}/MWh-thermal. We round to $0.20$.
\item \textbf{$P_{\text{gas}}$}. We pull from ESIOS indicator $1940$ (\emph{Precio Gas Natural TNP}) with magnitud \texteuro/ton, and convert using the IPCC default LHV $48$ TJ/Gg $= 13.33$ MWh/ton. \textbf{Caveat}: TNP is the Spanish gas-tariff hub; the relevant strategic opportunity cost is actually MIBGAS day-ahead PVB. Check the basis: if non-trivial we should switch to MIBGAS DA. For 2024--26 the TNP--MIBGAS-PVB wedge is typically small ($\lesssim 2$ EUR/MWh-th) but not zero.
\item \textbf{$P_{\text{CO}_2}$}. ESIOS indicator $1391$ (\emph{Precio derechos emisión CO2 PCO2D}), \texteuro/tCO\textsubscript{2}, monthly.
\item \textbf{$\mathrm{VOM} = 4$ \texteuro/MWh-elec --- genuinely conservative}. IEA \emph{Projected Costs} 2020 gives CCGT VOM in the 1--4 USD/MWh range. We use the upper bound (which \emph{shrinks} the computed premium).
\item \textbf{Start-up / no-load costs are NOT in the formula.} A CCGT cold-start is $\sim$\texteuro 1--20k per MW of nameplate; amortised across the running hours of a typical $\sim 100$-h block this adds $\sim 5$--$15$ EUR/MWh-elec on top of MC, especially for units called specifically for Fase~I secondary regulation rather than for bulk DA dispatch. The headline ``+58 to +68" premium below therefore likely overstates true rent by $\sim 10$ EUR/MWh; a tighter ``true rent" estimate is closer to $+45$ to $+55$ in DA15/ID15.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Identification note on ESIOS id 705.} ESIOS indicator 705 (\emph{Precio medio restricciones Fase I subir}) is the \textbf{accepted-energy pay-as-bid price} (\texteuro/MWh delivered), the average across all Fase~I-up subasta acceptances on the day. It is \emph{not} a capacity / band reservation price (those live in the aFRR-reserve series id 634/2130, ``banda secundaria"). The opportunity-cost benchmark is therefore the unit's SRMC, which is what we compute here. The OMIE / OS definition is in REE PO 3.2 read with CNMC Resolución 8 Sep 2022 settling Fase~I.

\textbf{Result.} Monthly aggregates with the central parameters give CCGT marginal cost in the range \texteuro 100--130/MWh-elec across the reform-window months, against DA spot \texteuro 53--120/MWh and Fase~I-up pay-as-bid \texteuro 145--261/MWh (Tables~\ref{tab:cascade_prices_up}--\ref{tab:bidshape_da}):

DA spot in 2024--2026 sits at or below CCGT MC much of the time.

\textbf{Two readings of the DA-bid-above-MC pattern (descriptive flag).} A DA bid above MC is rationalisable as either (i) competitive non-clearing (unit bids its MC and the market clears below it) or (ii) strategic inframarginal markup (unit bids above MC anticipating MCP $>$ MC). The DA bid pattern by itself does not distinguish between these. In contrast, Fase~I is pay-as-bid: any accepted bid above MC produces a positive cash flow per MWh delivered, irrespective of whether the unit is marginal or inframarginal. Reading prices against MC is therefore methodologically cleaner in Fase~I than in DA.

The $+45$ to $+55$ EUR/MWh figure is the average premium \emph{for the units that clear Fase~I}, not for ``Spanish CCGTs'' in general. Zonally-pivotal units are $\sim 20$--$30\%$ of the fleet; the descriptive scope of the figure is restricted to those units in voltage-fragile zones.

This complements the capacity-based concentration evidence (Table~\ref{tab:zonal_concentration}) with a bid-side observation: dominance is exercised. The CNMC's 2019 Naturgy sanction precedent (\S\ref{sec:prov:cournot_cnmc:sanctions}) covered exactly the GN southern-corridor plants whose flat \texteuro 840 ladder is documented here.

\section{System cost cascade: the full ajuste view}\label{sec:prov:full_cascade}
% =====================================================================

\textbf{Why this matters.} The DA and IDA prices in Part A cover only the front end of the market. Every MWh that clears in DA/IDA also triggers downstream pay-as-bid (or marginal) settlements in $\ge 6$ ancillary processes whose \emph{combined} cost can rival or exceed the DA energy bill in expensive periods (post-blackout). When we read MTU15-DA as a ``reduction in within-hour rigidity'', the falsifier is the cascade: if reform shifts cost rather than removing it, total system cost would not move. Empirically (this section) total cascade cost \emph{rose} under the reform window, with a large reallocation from real-time TR toward pre-IDA Fase~I.

\subsection{Service inventory}\label{sec:prov:full_cascade:inventory}

REE's adjustment markets, in order of pre-physical-delivery time-stamp (REE \emph{Guía de Proveedor de Servicios de Ajuste} v5.0, Dec 2024; PO 3.x for restrictions, PO 7.x for balance, PO 14 for settlement):
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item \textbf{Mercado diario (DA)} --- the OMIE auction in Part A.
\item \textbf{Restricciones Fase~I (PDBF)} --- between DA and IDA, \emph{tipo redespacho} 23 in archive 28. PO 3.2. Pay-as-bid; clears \emph{before} IDA opens. Resolves security restrictions on PDBF (Fase~1 of PO 3.2 — modification by security criteria); Fase~2 of PO 3.2 (generation/demand re-equilibrium) carries through.
\item \textbf{IDAs} --- 3 European intraday auctions and continuous XBID, as in Part A.
\item \textbf{Restricciones Fase~II (post-IDA)} --- residual rebalancing, tipo 24. Operationally dead post-2024 ($\le 7$ GWh per regime).
\item \textbf{Gestión de Desvíos (GD)} --- hourly between IDAs and balancing-gate closure (PO 7.3), tipo 92.
\item \textbf{Restricciones Tiempo Real (TR)} --- continuous post-gate-closure (PO 3.2 \S4.2), tipos $\in \{61, 65, 66, 68, 69\}$.
\item \textbf{Banda secundaria (aFRR capacity)} --- daily reserve auction for next-day quarter-hourly periods, offers before 16h D-1, marginal price clearing (REE \emph{Guía} §6.2). Spain-local market: $\sim 600$ MW reserved each direction.
\item \textbf{aFRR energy} --- PICASSO platform (European); marginal price per control cycle. Spain connected after IGCC (imbalance netting) in October 2020.
\item \textbf{mFRR (Regulación terciaria)} --- MARI platform since December 2024; activación programada (every 15 min) + activación directa (on demand). Marginal price per cycle.
\item \textbf{RR (Reserva de Sustitución)} --- TERRE platform since March 2020; marginal price per cycle.
\item \textbf{SRAD (Servicio de Respuesta Activa de la Demanda)} --- Spain-specific demand-response product, yearly subasta + per-event activation at mFRR price; small volume.
\item \textbf{Desvíos (imbalance settlement)} --- PO 14.4 dual-price true-up against MCP, per ISP, end of day D.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{FCR (Regulación primaria)} is mandatory and unpaid in Spain (no price indicator). \textbf{Control de tensión} (voltage / reactive support, PO 7.4) became a new paid product in mid-2025 (BOE RDL 7/2025; permanent rule BOE-A-2026-1377); ESIOS indicators are still in stand-up phase. The full code-by-code reading of \emph{tipo redespacho} is in the project reference (\texttt{docs/notes/SPANISH\_MARKET\_STRUCTURE.md} §13) and was applied to Figure~\ref{tab:phf_pdbf_alltech} above.

\textit{Reading the cascade.}
\begin{enumerate}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item \textbf{Total ajuste cost \emph{rose} under the reform.} From \texteuro 16.1M/day (ISP15-win) to \textbf{\texteuro 21.1M/day} (DA15/ID15), a $+31\%$ jump.
\item \textbf{The reform redistributed cost from TR to Fase~I.} Fase~I (up) cost went from \texteuro 5.3M/day to \texteuro 13.0M/day ($+145\%$); TR (up) cost went from \texteuro 5.8M/day to \texteuro 1.8M/day ($-69\%$). Net: $+\texteuro 5.7$M/day in Fase~I, $-\texteuro 4.0$M/day in TR. The reform \emph{pre-programmed} the post-clearing redispatch (predictable Fase~I CCGTs in the southern corridor) and reduced the need for real-time fixes.
\item \textbf{The post-blackout regime is the most expensive year on year}: DA60/ID15 post-blk $=$ \texteuro 17.7M/day. Fase~I jumped to \texteuro 10.5M/day already \emph{before} MTU15-DA, and TR-down compensation went strongly negative ($-\texteuro 66$M total) reflecting RES-curtailment payments during the operational-reforzada window.
\item \textbf{TR price halved} from ISP15-win (\texteuro 545/MWh) to DA15/ID15 (\texteuro 226/MWh) --- consistent with MTU15-IDA absorbing intra-hour scarcity that previously bled into TR.
\item \textbf{Fase~I price barely budged} (\texteuro 187 $\to$ \texteuro 168) but Fase~I \emph{cost} more than doubled. The volume from the project's per-cell RP48 code-23 series fell over the same window (Fase~I subir, codes from the \emph{tipo redespacho} taxonomy), so the implied per-MWh average is higher under MTU15-DA. The ESIOS cost indicator 1373 captures the \emph{full} Fase~I cost, including the substitution-cost addition (when the displaced DA program is much cheaper than the Fase~I winner) and Fase~I sub-categories beyond the RP48 code-23 series whose volume grew under reform.
\item \textbf{aFRR reserve (capacity)} is steady at $\sim \texteuro 1$--$3$M/day. The capacity-band auction is largely insulated from the energy-market reform.
\item \textbf{The TR-down (and RES-curtailment) compensation sign-flip} is real money flowing \emph{out} of the system to RES units for forgone production. Small share of total cost ($<3\%$) but visible.

\item \textbf{Seasonality control via Fourier-deseasonalization confirms the reform-window pattern but sharpens the TR collapse.} The bracketed {\color{seasoncol}values} are the regime's expected per-day cost at \emph{average day-of-year}, controlling for the seasonal cycle in the cost series. For \emph{Fase~I up}, the DA15/ID15 raw (\texteuro 13.0M/day) and adjusted (\texteuro 13.5M/day) values are nearly identical — Fase~I has a moderate winter peak and DA15/ID15 covers Oct--May, so the raw mean barely needs adjustment. The reform-window jump from ISP15-win adjusted \texteuro 4.5M/day to DA15/ID15 adjusted \texteuro 13.5M/day (a $\times 3$ increase) is the genuine post-reform shift. For \emph{TR up}, the DA15/ID15 raw \texteuro 1.8M/day adjusts \emph{down} to \texteuro 0.8M/day — the cost collapse is \emph{sharper} after seasonality removal because the raw winter-leaning months of DA15/ID15 pulled the average up. \textbf{Economic intuition}: Fase~I cost is driven by REE's pre-IDA security analysis, which scales with peak demand; both winter and reform contribute additively, and the deseasonalization separates them. TR cost is driven by within-hour scarcity, which the MTU15-IDA reform absorbs structurally; the seasonal-mix in the DA15/ID15 window slightly masked the true magnitude of the structural TR collapse, and the adjusted value (\texteuro 0.8M/day vs \texteuro 5.8M/day in ISP15-win adjusted) shows a 7$\times$ reduction.
\end{enumerate}

\textit{Implication for the DA / IDA findings in Part A.} The cascade evidence shows that MTU15 is \emph{not} purely a cost-saving reform: it \textbf{shifts cost from real-time to pre-IDA Fase~I}, with a net $\sim 30\%$ increase in per-day ancillary spending. Three corollaries: (i) apparent ``CCGT redispatch'' under reforzada (Table~\ref{tab:phf_pdbf_alltech}) is fundamentally a \emph{Fase~I} channel, not a TR one; the bulk of CCGT compensation flows through pay-as-bid Fase~I, where southern-corridor incumbents (\S\ref{sec:prov:geo}) face minimal effective competition. (ii) The \texteuro 21M/day ajuste bill in DA15/ID15 is an order of magnitude larger than any margin we could detect from DA price-setter analysis (\S\ref{sec:prov:pricesetter_da}); welfare counterfactuals that focus only on the DA auction will severely understate scarcity rents. (iii) The TR-channel reduction is real and is the \emph{efficient} part of the reform.

\paragraph{Reading the efficiency gains.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{ISP15 is the cleanest reform identification.} Outcome = absolute imbalance volume per ISP cell; the 60min $\to$ 15min settlement granularity mechanically shrinks per-cell variance by $\sim 4\times$ (independence bound), so we'd expect $\sim 50\%$ reduction in mean absolute value purely from the change of measurement. The observed reduction is $\sim 60\%$. The residual $\sim 10$ pp is the behavioural response. The widening of the dual-price spread is the strongest piece of evidence: cobro/pago move apart by $30/25$ EUR/MWh, making the settlement materially more punitive for being on the wrong side of system imbalance. There is no overlapping reform in the Dec 2024--Mar 2025 window (pre-MTU15-IDA, pre-reforzada), so this is the cleanest single-shock identification we have.

\item \textbf{MTU15-IDA delivered the big TR-cost channel.} TR cost dropped from \texteuro 5.8M to \texteuro 2.9M per day --- the largest single efficiency gain in the full cascade. Mechanism: granular IDA absorbs intra-hour scarcity that previously bled into real-time post-clearing intervention. TR price halved precisely because the residual demand for real-time correction shrank.

\item \textbf{MTU15-DA delivered a smaller marginal TR + aFRR efficiency channel, but was contemporaneous with reforzada-driven Fase~I cost growth.} The marginal TR savings (an additional \texteuro 1.1M/day) and the aFRR-capacity price collapse are clean MTU15-DA effects: nothing in the operational-reforzada policy mechanism would drive these. The Fase~I cost growth (\texteuro 2.5M/day extra) is a SEPARATE policy effect from reforzada (tighter PO-3.x security criteria force more CCGTs on, paid pay-as-bid). The two are confounded in the post-Oct 2025 window but logically distinct. The net is +19\% per-day cost, but the decomposition is:
   \begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
   \item Genuine MTU15-DA efficiency channel: $-$\texteuro 1.1M/day (TR) $+$ smaller aFRR savings $\approx -$\texteuro 1.5M/day
   \item Reforzada cost growth (independent of MTU15-DA): $+$\texteuro 2.5M/day (Fase~I)
   \item Other components (Fase~II, RES curtailment): small
   \end{itemize}
   \textbf{The MTU15-DA reform did deliver an efficiency dividend, but it is swamped in the aggregate cost data by the parallel reforzada cost growth.} Separately identifying the two requires either the 40-day DA60/ID15 pre-blk window (post-MTU15-IDA, pre-reforzada) or a geographic/firm-level discontinuity (non-reforzada zones, non-required CCGTs).
\end{itemize}

\textit{Bottom line on efficiency.} Of the three reforms:
\begin{enumerate}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item \textbf{ISP15} (settlement) is the cleanest, and the gain is unambiguous --- tighter within-hour discipline.
\item \textbf{MTU15-IDA} is the biggest single channel: $-$\texteuro 2.9M/day in TR cost.
\item \textbf{MTU15-DA} delivers genuine TR $+$ aFRR savings of $\sim$\texteuro 1.5M/day but is dominated in raw aggregates by the parallel operational-reforzada policy.
\end{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Solar PV CI activity is moderate and roughly stable in net terms.} Solar PV net CI position oscillates around zero ($-0.3$ to $+0.1$ GWh/day) across regimes; the technology uses CI for both incremental sales (over-forecast hours) and buy-backs (under-forecast hours) without a structural directional bias.

The CI volume reduction in the MTU15-IDA-only window is the empirical face of the TR-channel savings documented in Table~\ref{tab:efficiency_gains}: granular IDA absorbs the scarcity that previously fell to XBID and to real-time TR. The CI recovery under MTU15-DA tracks the new granular DA generating more per-quarter rebalancing demand.

\section{Within-hour variance and ISP15 settlement exposure}\label{sec:prov:isp15_per_tech}
% =====================================================================

\textbf{Why this matters.} The ISP15 settlement reform (60-min $\to$ 15-min ISP, effective 2024-12-01) was previously documented in \S\ref{sec:prov:full_cascade:efficiency} at the system level: average absolute imbalance volume fell $\sim 60\%$ from $\sim$810 MW/hour to $\sim$325 MW/ISP, with the dual-price spread widening from \texteuro 40/85 to \texteuro 70/110 per MWh. The aggregate read is that ISP15 made the settlement markedly more punitive. The differential question this section addresses is \emph{across which technologies} the settlement burden of ISP15 falls. Under the old ISP60, within-hour deviations of any technology's actual generation from its hourly schedule averaged out within the hour and produced zero settlement load. Under ISP15, each 15-minute cell is settled separately, so the within-hour variance of generation becomes a direct per-MWh imbalance exposure.

\textbf{Variable.} For each (date, clock-hour, technology) we compute $\sigma_{\text{within}}(d, h, \text{tech})$ as the within-hour standard deviation of the four 15-minute actual-generation values from ENTSO-E A75 (per PSR type, 15-min ISP resolution). The per-regime mean $\sigma_{\text{within}}$ in MW measures the structural within-hour ``churn'' of each technology. Implied monthly imbalance-exposure cost per technology is the product $\sigma_{\text{within}} \times \text{spread}_{\text{regime}} \times 720~\text{hours/month}$, where the spread is the per-regime dual-price spread (mean of cobro-up and pago-dn). \textbf{Sources}: ENTSO-E A75 \texttt{gen\_actual\_per\_type\_all.parquet}; spreads from ESIOS indicators 686 / 687 reproduced in \S\ref{sec:prov:full_cascade:efficiency}.

\textit{Reading.}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
\item \textbf{Solar PV bears the largest absolute exposure.} Within-hour $\sigma_w$ for solar PV is $\sim 660$ MW under DA15/ID15 (7.6\% of its 8.6 GW mean) --- the highest in MW terms of any technology. The implied monthly imbalance-exposure cost rises from \texteuro 25M/month under pre-ISP15 settlement to \texteuro 50M/month under DA15/ID15, a near-doubling. This is the channel through which ISP15 falls hardest on solar: large within-hour swings (cloud-cover transitions, especially during morning ramp and evening fade) are now settled on the 15-min ISP grid rather than netted within the hour.

\item \textbf{Hydro pumped storage has the largest \emph{relative} variance.} Pumped storage $\sigma_w/\bar{Q}$ runs $\sim 19\%$ (vs $\sim 7.6\%$ for solar PV) --- the highest variance fraction of any large-capacity technology. Total MW are smaller (1.6 GW mean), so absolute exposure is modest at \texteuro 23M/month under DA15/ID15, but the technology operates at high variance by design (pump $\leftrightarrow$ turbine switching). ISP15 increases its per-MWh settlement burden mechanically.

\item \textbf{Wind onshore is the lowest-variance large RES.} Wind $\sigma_w/\bar{Q} \approx 2.1\%$ --- much smaller than solar PV. Wind generation is structurally smoother than solar within the hour (no analogue of cloud-cover transitions or solar-fade ramps at sub-hour timescales), so wind's per-MW imbalance exposure is roughly $4\times$ smaller than solar PV at the same generation level. The monthly exposure rises from \texteuro 7M to \texteuro 12M between pre-ISP15 and DA15/ID15 ($\times 1.75$).

\item \textbf{Nuclear is near-flat by design.} Nuclear $\sigma_w/\bar{Q} \approx 0.37\%$, the smallest in the panel. Baseload operation means within-hour variance is dominated by trip / curtailment events rather than by structural generation profile. Implied monthly exposure is small (\texteuro 1--2M).

\item \textbf{CCGT $\sigma_w/\bar{Q} \approx 3.4\%$, intermediate.} CCGT plants ramp under load-following control with $\sigma_w \sim 200$ MW at $\sim 6$ GW mean dispatch. Monthly exposure rises from \texteuro 8M (pre-ISP15) to \texteuro 16M (DA15/ID15), almost doubling. The CCGT exposure is structural (ramp-following) plus driven by the post-clearing redispatch documented in \S\ref{sec:prov:phf_minus_pdbf}.

\item \textbf{The renewable-penalty channel is concentrated in solar PV, not wind.} Across the table, the absolute monthly-exposure increase from pre-ISP15 to DA15/ID15 is \texteuro 25M for solar PV vs only \texteuro 5M for wind. Wind onshore is structurally smoother and falls in the same bracket as CCGT in per-MWh imbalance exposure. The ``ISP15 penalises renewables more'' framing in the energy-policy discussion is specifically a \emph{solar} channel under our measurement: $\sigma_w$ for solar PV is $\sim 4\times$ that of wind at similar installed bases.
\end{itemize}

\textit{Verdict.} ISP15 settlement makes per-MWh imbalance exposure proportional to within-hour generation variance. Solar PV has by far the largest within-hour variance in absolute MW terms (cloud-cover ramps at sub-hour timescales) and therefore bears the largest absolute monthly exposure under the post-ISP15 settlement regime. Wind onshore, despite the policy-discussion framing as ``the variable RES'', is structurally much smoother and faces an exposure profile closer to dispatchable thermal plants. Hydro pumped storage has the highest variance \emph{fraction} but small absolute scale. Nuclear and biomass face negligible exposure. The cross-tech ranking is broadly stable across regimes; what changes between pre- and post-ISP15 is the dual-price spread that multiplies the variance into monetary exposure --- the channel by which the cost falls on RES is the price wedge, not the variance itself.

\emph{Caveat (measurement scope).} The exposure estimates above use $\sigma_w \times \text{spread}$ as the \emph{theoretical per-MWh exposure}, multiplied by 720 hours/month. The actual ESIOS-published imbalance volumes (id 762, system-level) move from $\sim$810 MW/hour pre-ISP15 to $\sim$325 MW/ISP post-ISP15 (\S\ref{sec:prov:full_cascade:efficiency}); the implied system-wide imbalance settlement is therefore not directly recoverable from these per-tech variance numbers without a within-BRP netting adjustment, which is not available at unit-level. The interpretation in the table is comparative across techs, not absolute aggregate.

\section{RES capture rate (\emph{apuntamiento})}\label{sec:prov:apuntamiento}
% =====================================================================

\textbf{Variable.} For a technology $\tau$, the apuntamiento or \emph{capture rate} is
\[
\mathrm{App}_\tau = \frac{\sum_h q_{\tau h} \cdot p_h^{\text{DA}}}{\sum_h q_{\tau h}} \, \Big/ \, \frac{1}{|H|}\sum_h p_h^{\text{DA}}
= \frac{\text{generation-weighted DA price}}{\text{arithmetic-mean DA price}}.
\]

$\mathrm{App}_\tau < 1$ means the technology earns \emph{less} than the average DA price (it is a price-impact recipient: its own abundance depresses prices in the hours when it produces). $\mathrm{App}_\tau > 1$ means it earns \emph{more} than average (it produces preferentially in peak hours). This is the standard cannibalisation diagnostic and the empirical content of CNMC's claim that ``RES are price-takers in aggregate''.

\textbf{Computation.} OMIE \texttt{marginalpdbc} (DA spot, full coverage 2023--2026) joined to ENTSO-E A75 actual generation per type, on the full hour grid (left-join, zero-fill missing generation hours so the night hours of Solar PV are correctly counted with $q = 0$ in the denominator). Note: ESIOS indicator 600 has month gaps in 2025--2026 and an earlier inner-join version of this calculation was biased upward by both the data gap and the missing night-hour rows. The corrected version below uses OMIE DA $+$ the full hour grid.

\subsection{Cournot reading of the cap-padded ladder}\label{sec:prov:cournot_cnmc:cournot}

A near-constant DA bid price ladder at the cap (GN at $\sim 840$ EUR/MWh in Table~\ref{tab:bid_vs_rt2}) combined with all the adjustment on the \emph{quantity} side --- the unit either clears DA or it does not, but its bid price hardly varies --- is the empirical signature of a \textbf{Cournot strategy on the quantity margin}. In pure economic terms this is equivalent to direct price-raising in DA, but the regulatorily defensible posture for the firm is ``my offer price has not changed; my bids follow my published ladder.'' The redispatch channel (Fase~I pay-as-bid) is then where the per-MWh rent is captured.

\part{Part C --- Synthesis}\label{sec:prov:synthesis}
% =============================================================================

\noindent\emph{Reading guide.} This Part summarises in plain prose what the descriptive evidence in Parts A and B shows, so that a reader unfamiliar with the Spanish market can follow the mechanism and the firm-level incentives without rereading the technical sections. Numbers cited here are reproduced from the tables and figures above; section anchors are kept in case the reader wants to drill in.

% =====================================================================
\section{What this document studies}\label{sec:prov:synthesis:setting}
% =====================================================================

Between June 2024 and October 2025 the Spanish wholesale electricity market went through three sequential reforms that progressively shortened the unit of trading and settlement from one hour to fifteen minutes. The day-ahead auction, the intraday auctions, the continuous intraday platform, and the imbalance-settlement layer were all affected in different combinations and at different dates. Overlaid on this reform sequence are two other shocks: the system-wide blackout of April 28, 2025, and the reinforced-operation stance (\emph{operación reforzada}) that the system operator REE adopted in its aftermath, which mandates the physical availability of additional combined-cycle gas (CCGT) units in voltage-fragile zones regardless of whether they cleared the day-ahead market.

This document is a descriptive companion to the thesis. It does not estimate average treatment effects of any of the three reforms. It documents bidding patterns (Part A), the regulatory and cost cascade that surrounds them (Part B), and provides the institutional context that any subsequent identification strategy must rest on. The regressions and statistics that appear in the text are instrumental --- they serve to characterise patterns, not to deliver causal effects.

% =====================================================================
\section{Headline empirical phenomenon}\label{sec:prov:synthesis:phenomenon}
% =====================================================================

The headline fact, documented in \S\ref{sec:prov:q1_drop} and reinforced throughout Part B, is that the volume of electricity supplied by CCGT plants of the four largest Spanish firms through the day-ahead auction collapsed by roughly $80\%$ between late 2024 and late 2025 (Table~\ref{tab:q1_drop_by_tech}: 1{,}444 GWh in December 2024 against 244 GWh in December 2025, in the same calendar month). Every other technology --- nuclear, hydro, wind, solar PV, pumped storage --- either grew or stayed flat over the same window. The collapse is therefore specific to CCGT, and specific to the Big-4 incumbents (Iberdrola, Endesa, Naturgy, Naturgy's successor entities, and EDP). \emph{\color{seasoncol}The headline number is calendar-on-calendar (Dec 2024 vs Dec 2025) by construction, so seasonality plays no role in the $-83\%$ figure.}

What happened to the production that left the day-ahead auction is not that the CCGTs stopped running. The difference between the post-intraday dispatch program (PHF) and the day-ahead-plus-bilateral baseline (PDBF) --- the volume that REE adds back after the auctions clear --- rose by roughly $1{,}000$ GWh per month for CCGT alone in the post-blackout window (Table~\ref{tab:gap_pretrends}), while every other technology lost volume on the same channel. Bilateral contracts cancel out in this difference and the per-firm decomposition (Table~\ref{tab:phf_pdbf_alltech}) confirms that every Big-4 CCGT firm at least doubles its post-clearing gap; Naturgy alone gains $+534$ GWh per month. The CCGT output is simply being absorbed elsewhere in the program chain. \emph{\color{seasoncol}The post-DA gap increase survives Fourier-deseasonalization: the daily CCGT post-DA gap rises from $\sim$47 GWh/day (3-sess baseline) to $\sim$389 GWh/day (MTU15-IDA post-blackout) at average day-of-year, the structural break is preserved.}

% =====================================================================
\section{The mechanism, step by step}\label{sec:prov:synthesis:mechanism}
% =====================================================================

The decoupling of where CCGTs offer (day-ahead) and where they are paid (REE redispatch) makes the day-ahead auction a non-binding step for these units. The chain reads as follows:

\begin{enumerate}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
\item \textbf{The unit stays out of day-ahead.} The CCGT bids at or near the technical price cap (\S\ref{sec:prov:bid_vs_rt2}: Naturgy at $\sim 840$ EUR/MWh, near-constant across days). At those prices the unit does not clear the auction. The bid price is invariant, so the firm can plausibly argue ``we always offered the same price; the market did not clear us.'' Adjustment happens on the quantity margin, not the price margin. This is the empirical signature of a Cournot strategy implemented through quantity withholding (\S\ref{sec:prov:cournot_cnmc:cournot}). \emph{\color{seasoncol}The €840 plateau is observed across calendar months in both 2024 and 2025 (Table~\ref{tab:bid_by_zonal_dominance}'s pre vs post comparison is same-calendar by construction), so it is not a seasonal artifact.}

\item \textbf{REE recalls the unit through the post-clearing redispatch.} The reinforced-operation stance requires that certain CCGTs be physically available in the southern corridor (Sur, Levante, Cataluña) and Galicia for voltage support and contingency cover (\S\ref{sec:prov:geo}). Once the day-ahead auction has cleared without those units, REE adds them back through the Phase~I post-clearing redispatch market and through real-time technical restrictions.

\item \textbf{Phase~I is pay-as-bid.} Unlike the day-ahead auction (which clears at a single marginal price), Phase~I accepts each offer at its own submitted price. A CCGT in a voltage-fragile zone that REE has to call faces effectively no competition: its bid sets its own payment. The Phase~I-up price averaged $\texteuro 168$ per MWh in the most recent regime, against a bottom-up estimate of CCGT short-run marginal cost in the $\texteuro 100$--$110$ range (Table~\ref{tab:mc_ccgt_premium}). The implied mark-up of roughly $30$--$60\%$ over marginal cost is captured pay-as-bid by zonally-dominant units. After adjusting for start-up amortisation the rent estimate narrows to $+45$ to $+55$ EUR/MWh.

\item \textbf{Zonal dominance closes the loop.} Whether a firm can exploit the chain depends on whether it operates the CCGT that REE has to call in a given zone. In the Sur, Levante, and Cataluña corridors that map drives a single firm to capture most of the rent: Naturgy collects roughly $50\%$ of the Big-4's post-clearing CCGT revenue in every regime (Table~\ref{tab:ccgt_post_clearing_rev}), corresponding to about $\texteuro 2$ million per day pre-reforzada and $\texteuro 5$--$8$ million per day post-reforzada.
\end{enumerate}

This is not novel conduct. The 2019 CNMC sanction SNC-DE-175-17 (Table~\ref{tab:cnmc_sanctions}) found Naturgy guilty of exactly the same practice --- ``abnormal day-ahead offers'' at plants in the southern corridor --- over October 2016 to January 2017, with an estimated benefit of \texteuro 13 million over four months. The 2024--26 evidence in this document is the same practice continuing, now operating at fleet-wide scale and leveraging the new fifteen-minute granularity as an additional instrument.

\section{What this implies for the cost cascade and the reforms}\label{sec:prov:synthesis:cascade}
% =====================================================================

The total per-day cost of the ajuste services REE pays for to keep the system feasible rose from \texteuro 16.1 million per day under ISP15-winter to \texteuro 21.1 million per day under the most recent regime (Table in \S\ref{sec:prov:full_cascade:perday}), a $+31\%$ increase. The composition of the increase is the diagnostic part: Phase~I cost rose $+145\%$ (\texteuro 5.3M $\to$ \texteuro 13.0M per day) while real-time technical-restrictions cost fell $-69\%$ (\texteuro 5.8M $\to$ \texteuro 1.8M per day). REE is paying CCGTs more, but earlier in the program chain (pre-IDA Phase~I) rather than in real-time emergency redispatch. The retail-side decomposition of the PVPC regulated tariff (\S\ref{sec:prov:full_cascade:pvpc}) confirms the same picture from the consumer side: the Phase~I component nearly triples and the real-time component falls by half.

\emph{\color{seasoncol}The cascade redistribution survives Fourier-deseasonalization.} The Phase~I rise from ISP15-winter to DA15/ID15 on adjusted values is \texteuro 4.5M $\to$ \texteuro 13.5M per day (a $\times 3$ increase) and the TR collapse is \texteuro 5.8M $\to$ \texteuro 0.8M per day (a $\times 7$ reduction; the raw collapse from \texteuro 5.8M to \texteuro 1.8M understates the structural channel because DA15/ID15 raw is winter-leaning and partly seasonal). Both reform-window patterns hold direction and magnitude after seasonality removal.

Of the three reforms, the descriptive evidence supports three different verdicts (\S\ref{sec:prov:full_cascade:efficiency}):

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
\item \textbf{ISP15 (settlement granularity, December 2024)} is the cleanest reform identification we have. The outcome variable --- absolute imbalance volume per settlement cell --- moved by about $60\%$, of which roughly $50\%$ is the mechanical variance-reduction effect of finer-grained measurement and the residual $\sim 10$ percentage points is a behavioural response. The widening of the dual-price spread (the asymmetric reward/penalty for being on the right/wrong side of system imbalance) supports the behavioural reading.

\item \textbf{MTU15-IDA (intraday clearing granularity, March 2025)} delivered the largest single efficiency channel: real-time technical-restriction cost fell from \texteuro 5.8M per day to \texteuro 2.9M per day. The mechanism is that finer-grained intraday clearing absorbs within-hour scarcity that would previously have spilled over into real-time restriction. The real-time restriction price approximately halved.

\item \textbf{MTU15-DA (day-ahead clearing granularity, October 2025)} delivered an additional but smaller efficiency dividend (a further \texteuro 1.1M per day in real-time savings and a roughly $50\%$ reduction in the capacity-reserve price) but was implemented contemporaneously with the reinforced-operation policy that drove Phase~I cost up by $+\texteuro 2.5M$ per day. The two effects are confounded in the aggregate data. The descriptive evidence is consistent with a positive but modest efficiency gain swamped by the parallel cost growth in the redispatch channel.
\end{itemize}

\section{What the bidding-pattern evidence in Part A adds}\label{sec:prov:synthesis:partA}
% =====================================================================

Part A characterises the bid-curve shape itself. The first and methodologically distinct result is the granularity outcome proper: the within-hour quarter dissimilarity ($D_w$, $\Delta p$, $\Delta q$ in \S\ref{sec:prov:dw_decomp}--\S\ref{sec:prov:dw_tables}). This is the dimension MTU15 actually opened up --- the ability to bid different prices and quantities across the four 15-minute quarters within a clock hour. The correct test of whether firms are ``exploiting MTU15'' is whether they exercise this within-hour discrimination, not whether their bid-curve concentrates around the clearing price (a separate per-period measure).

\textbf{The headline within-hour result: most firms do not exercise the within-hour margin even after the reform.} Table~\ref{tab:dw_mass_median} (DA Oct--Dec 2025, the post-MTU15-DA window) shows that the share of (firm, unit, date, hour) cells with $D_w = 0$ --- zero within-hour shaping --- is essentially universal for three of the four Big-4 CCGT firms: IB 98\% critical / 98\% flat, GE 98\% / 100\%, HC 100\% / 100\%. The granular day-ahead auction is not used by these firms. Only \textbf{GN} shows substantial activity: $77\%$ $D_w = 0$ in critical hours and $100\%$ in flat. From the channel decomposition (Table~\ref{tab:dw_decomp}), GN's activity is concentrated entirely on the \emph{quantity} margin --- $18.4\%$ of critical cells are $\Delta q$-active versus $0.01\%$ $\Delta p$-active --- which is the Cournot signature: keep the bid price ladder fixed near the cap, reallocate quantity across the within-hour quarters. GE shows a small price-channel signal ($1.56\%$ critical $\Delta p$-active), an order of magnitude smaller than GN's quantity channel.

\textbf{The critical-flat asymmetry is essentially absolute.} For every firm, every regime, and every market (DA or IDA), flat-hour within-hour shaping is $\sim 0\%$. GN: $100\%$ of flat-hour cells have $D_w = 0$. GE, HC: same. IB: $0\%$ $\Delta q$-active in flat hours. This is the validation of the hour-class taxonomy (\S\ref{sec:prov:hour_class}): granular pricing has economic content only where within-hour residual demand actually moves; flat hours show no shaping because there is nothing to shape against, and the critical-only activity confirms that what we observe in critical hours is strategic exploitation of the within-hour residual-demand structure, not noise.

\textbf{Per reform, what each MTU15 step could and did move:}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
\item \textbf{ISP15 (Dec 2024)} is settlement-side; it cannot affect within-hour bid dissimilarity because both DA and IDA auctions were still hourly. The within-hour outcome is mechanically zero for the entire ISP15 window. ISP15's effects are on settlement discipline (dual-price-spread widening) and on the system imbalance volume, not on auction-side within-hour bidding.
\item \textbf{MTU15-IDA (Mar 2025)} made IDA bids per-quarter. The share of CCGT cells with $D_w = 0$ in IDA falls substantially post-blackout for every firm: GN $70\% \to 46\%$ in critical, GE $86\% \to 56\%$, IB $90\% \to 80\%$, HC $72\% \to 66\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:dw_mass_median}). GN's within-hour activity in IDA roughly doubles. Confounded with reforzada, but the direction is unambiguous: the IDA reform enabled real within-hour shaping by all Big-4 CCGT firms.
\item \textbf{MTU15-DA (Oct 2025)} made DA bids per-quarter. Only GN exercises this in the day-ahead market ($23\%$ of critical cells with within-hour activity, all on the quantity margin). GE, IB, HC show negligible exploitation.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{The bid-curve concentration around MCP (Tables~\ref{tab:bidshape_da}, \ref{tab:bidshape_ida}) is a separate metric.} It captures how tight the bid stack is around the clearing price, not whether it differs across within-hour quarters. We document it because it is the standard tightness-of-supply measure in the literature and it does respond to the reforms on Fourier-deseasonalized values --- CCGT critical-hour adjusted share rises from $\sim 23\%$ to $\sim 41\%$ across the reform window. But this is bid-curve concentration, not within-hour granularity exploitation; the two outcomes can move independently, and the granularity-outcome-proper is the within-hour dissimilarity above.

The functional principal-component analysis (\S\ref{sec:prov:fpca}) is consistent with both: PC1 (high-price tail) moves across regimes (the bid-curve concentration story) and placebo comparisons against 2022 and 2023 calendar matches do not reproduce the movement, confirming the reform-window pattern is not a seasonal recurrence.

\section{Seasonality robustness: what survives, what does not}\label{sec:prov:synthesis:seasonality}
% =====================================================================

The reform-window comparisons in this document use windows that span different calendar months: 3-sess is Jun--Nov 2024, ISP15-win is Dec 2024 -- Mar 2025, DA15/ID15 is Oct 2025 -- May 2026. Spanish electricity is strongly seasonal (heating demand in winter, solar peak in summer, wind peak in winter, hydro inflows in spring), so naive cross-regime comparisons confound the reform with the seasonal mix. We control for this throughout the document via two complementary mechanisms: \emph{(i)} where possible, same-calendar-month comparisons that hold the seasonal contribution fixed by construction (e.g., the q1-drop Dec-vs-Dec comparison in Table~\ref{tab:q1_drop_by_tech}, the bid-by-zonal-dominance pre-vs-post in Table~\ref{tab:bid_by_zonal_dominance}); \emph{(ii)} a Frisch-Waugh-Lovell regression that pools the daily 2022--2026 series and recovers, for each regime, the predicted value of the outcome at \emph{average day-of-year}, after partialling out a $K=4$ Fourier basis on day-of-year and (slow-moving) covariates such as installed renewable capacity and reservoir filling. Fourier-deseasonalization is the standard approach in the electricity time-series literature; month or year fixed effects are not identified in our setting because regime windows do not span all calendar months. The {\color{seasoncol}bracketed} values throughout the document are the regime-specific predicted levels from this regression.

\textbf{The headline patterns survive seasonality removal.}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
\item \textbf{CCGT day-ahead auction-cleared collapse} ($-83\%$ Dec 2024 vs Dec 2025): same-calendar comparison, no seasonality role by construction.
\item \textbf{CCGT post-DA gap spike} ($+1{,}000$ GWh/month): Fourier-adjusted CCGT post-DA gap goes from $\sim 47$ GWh/day (3-sess baseline, at average day-of-year) to $\sim 389$ GWh/day (post-blackout MTU15-IDA), the structural break is preserved.
\item \textbf{CCGT bid-curve concentration near MCP}: critical-hour adjusted share rises from $\sim 23\%$ (3-sess) to $\sim 41\%$ (DA15/ID15) in DA, and from $\sim 54\%$ to $\sim 80\%$ in IDA. The cross-regime pattern is not a seasonal-mix artifact. \emph{Note: this is a per-period concentration metric, distinct from the within-hour quarter dissimilarity below.}
\item \textbf{Within-hour quarter dissimilarity ($D_w$, $\Delta p$, $\Delta q$ --- the granularity outcome proper):} the share of $D_w > 0$ cells in IDA rises substantially post-MTU15-IDA for every Big-4 CCGT firm; in DA only GN exercises the within-hour margin post-MTU15-DA ($23\%$ of critical cells, quantity-channel-only). The critical-flat asymmetry is absolute --- flat-hour within-hour shaping is essentially zero for every firm, validating the hour-class taxonomy. These outcomes are observed in single-regime samples and do not need seasonality adjustment.
\item \textbf{Cost-cascade redistribution} (Phase~I up, TR down): Phase~I rise from ISP15-winter to DA15/ID15 is \texteuro 4.5M $\to$ \texteuro 13.5M per day on adjusted values ($\times 3$); TR collapse is \texteuro 5.8M $\to$ \texteuro 0.8M per day on adjusted values ($\times 7$, sharper than the raw collapse from \texteuro 5.8M to \texteuro 1.8M because DA15/ID15 raw is winter-leaning and partly seasonal).
\item \textbf{Day-ahead spot price decline}: DA spot fell from \texteuro 117/MWh (ISP15-winter raw) to \texteuro 76/MWh (DA15/ID15 raw); on Fourier-adjusted values, \texteuro 95 $\to$ \texteuro 43/MWh. The post-reform price decline strengthens after deseasonalization.
\item \textbf{Continuous-intraday (XBID) total volume fell substantially under MTU15-IDA}: $\sim 30$ GWh/day (3-sess) $\to$ $\sim 21$ GWh/day (DA15/ID15) on deseasonalized values, with a trough at $\sim 11$ GWh/day during the 40-day MTU15-IDA-only window. The granular IDA absorbed scarcity that previously fell to XBID and to real-time TR. The decrease is mirrored in TR cost (above) and survives Fourier-deseasonalization in every cut (system, critical hours, flat hours, per tech).
\item \textbf{Per-firm within-hour rebalancing intensity in CI is preserved post-reform}: across MTU15-IDA pre/post and DA15/ID15, every Big-4 firm maintains roughly constant within-hour quarter std of CI trades (IB $\sim 12$ MWh, GN $\sim 9$, HC $\sim 7$, GE $\sim 3$ in critical hours, CV $\approx 0.3$--$0.5$). The total CI \emph{volume} fell, but firms still make differently-sized trades across the four within-hour quarters of an hour. \emph{Aggregating quarters into hourly totals would hide this entirely}.
\item \textbf{GN cap-padded bid ladder} (~\texteuro 840/MWh across all zones): the pre-vs-post comparison in Table~\ref{tab:bid_by_zonal_dominance} is calendar-matched, so the uniform-cap-ladder pattern is not seasonal. GE's $4\times$ dominant-vs-non-dominant zonal bid differential ($+\texteuro 1{,}344$ post) is likewise calendar-matched.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Where seasonality matters for interpretation.} For wind, solar PV, and nuclear bid-shape, the raw cross-regime levels are largely seasonal-mix artifacts: wind in-band share is mechanically inflated in high-wind seasons (and the 40-day MTU15-IDA pre-blackout window catches a late-March / April high-wind stretch); solar PV the same for summer-leaning regimes; nuclear is driven by spring / late-summer refuelling outages. The Fourier-adjusted columns of the bid-shape tables remove these artifacts. For these technologies, cross-regime claims should rely only on the {\color{seasoncol}adjusted} values.

\section{What we did not do, and why}\label{sec:prov:synthesis:scope}
% =====================================================================

The descriptive scope is deliberate. The three reforms are time-overlapping (the post-MTU15-IDA window is the same window where the reinforced-operation policy starts, with the only intersection being a 40-day pre-blackout sub-window of borderline statistical power), and the day-ahead clearing-granularity reform shares its window entirely with the most cost-intensive year of the reinforced-operation policy. Any single-equation estimate of ``the effect of MTU15-DA'' would, in this dataset, conflate the genuine clearing-granularity efficiency channel with the reinforced-operation cost growth that is logically independent of clearing granularity. We therefore characterise outcomes one at a time and document, for each, what the descriptive evidence supports and what it does not.

The CNMC sanction precedent and the new April-2026 expediente cohort (\S\ref{sec:prov:cournot_cnmc:sanctions}, \S\ref{sec:prov:cournot_cnmc:blackout}) provide the external regulatory check on the descriptive reading: the pattern documented here is the one Spanish regulators have already pursued as a market-conduct case in 2019 and are pursuing again at fleet scale in 2026.
